## The Synthesis Exercises

Work through these in order. They build on each other. Budget the full forty minutes.

---

### Exercise 1: The Single Stance (10 min)

Nineteen concepts. But they are not nineteen separate ideas. They are nineteen facets of one underlying stance.

In your own words — not a list, but a paragraph — describe that stance. What is the *one thing* that all nineteen concepts are expressions of?

Some prompts if you are stuck:
- What do all the perception concepts have in common about how they relate to imperfection, impermanence, and openness?
- What do all the practice concepts share about how they relate to engagement, effort, and direction?
- What do the integration concepts add that the first two arcs leave incomplete?
- If you had to explain this entire course to an intelligent friend in three minutes — no lists, no jargon — what would you say?

Write the paragraph. It does not need to be perfect. It needs to be honest.

---

### Exercise 2: The Personal Through-Line (10 min)

Go back to the reason you began this course — the specific thing you wanted to see differently, the opportunity within your current life you wanted to find and align with.

Now answer: of the nineteen concepts, which three have most changed how you see that specific thing? Not which three are most intellectually interesting — which three have actually altered your perception or orientation toward what you came here to address?

For each of the three:
- Name the concept
- Describe in one sentence how it has shifted your perception
- Give one specific example from your life that looks different through this lens than it did on Day 1

---

### Exercise 3: The Living Concepts (10 min)

Two further questions:

**A. Which concept is already living in you?**
Of all nineteen, which one has most quietly and naturally become part of how you see and act in daily life — without needing to consciously remember it? This is the concept that has moved from idea to instinct. Describe in two sentences what this looks like in practice.

**B. Which concept most resists you?**
Which one do you understand intellectually but cannot yet quite inhabit? What is the resistance? Is it that the concept asks something of you that is genuinely difficult? Or that you haven't yet found where it lives in your specific life? Write three sentences on the resistance — honest, specific, without judgement.

---

### Exercise 4: The Capstone Preparation (10 min)

Tomorrow you will write two things:
1. **Your Ikigai Map** — a narrative (not a diagram) of what makes you feel most alive, drawing on twenty days of observation
2. **Your Living Philosophy** — a single page (300–500 words) describing how you want to inhabit each day

Today, in preparation, write rough notes for both.

**For the Ikigai Map:** List the specific things — people, activities, conditions, qualities of engagement — that reliably produce the feeling of *it is good to be alive right now.* Be specific and honest. Include small things. Include things that might seem trivial. These are real data.

**For the Living Philosophy:** Finish these three sentences, roughly, in whatever words come:
- "I want to see my daily life as..."
- "I want to act in my work and relationships with..."
- "When difficulty comes, I want to..."

These are not the final sentences. They are the bones. Tomorrow you'll add the flesh.

---

### Exercise 5: The Question You Still Carry (5 min)

After nineteen days and twenty concepts, what is the question you are still sitting with — the one the course has opened rather than closed?

Write it down. This question is not a failure; it is the most honest signal of where your learning has gone deep enough to reveal its own edge. Carry it into Day 21. Carry it beyond.

---

## A Note on What "Synthesis" Means

Synthesis is not summary. A summary reduces content to a smaller version of itself. Synthesis produces something new — a *perspective* that sees how the parts form a whole. The difference between someone who has memorised nineteen concepts and someone who has synthesised them is not what they know; it is how they see.

You have been reading a course about perception. The final test is whether your perception has actually changed. Today's exercises are that test — not to pass or fail, but to honestly see where you are, so that tomorrow's capstone can come from that honest place.
