# Module 3 — Integration (Days 18–21)

> **What this module earns you:** A one-page living philosophy that is genuinely yours — assembled from twenty days of ancient ideas, shaped to the specific texture of your actual life.

---

## What This Module Is For

By now you have encountered nineteen distinct Japanese philosophical concepts. The risk at this point is fragmentation: a collection of interesting ideas with no through-line. This module resolves that risk.

The integration arc does two things:

1. **Days 18–19** add the final two concepts — mottainai (the ethics of enough) and gaman (enduring with dignity) — that complete the philosophical picture. These are not new ideas so much as the final angle on ideas you already know.

2. **Days 20–21** are the synthesis: a rest day to consolidate, then a capstone that forces you to use everything you've built.

---

## The Through-Line You've Been Building

Every concept in this course is an expression of one underlying stance: *a relationship with reality that moves with the grain of things rather than against them.*

- **Perception arc:** Seeing the world as it actually is — impermanent, imperfect, spacious, mysterious, unrepeatable, and open.
- **Practice arc:** Acting from that seeing — with aliveness, patient improvement, craftsman attention, dignified resilience, and freedom from comparison.
- **Integration arc:** Holding all of it as one thing — a life that wastes nothing, endures with grace, and knows its own shape.

Mottainai and gaman are the final two facets of that stance: how you relate to what you have (enough) and how you relate to difficulty (dignity).

## The Capstone: What "Done" Looks Like

The Day 21 capstone asks you to produce two things:

**1. Your Ikigai Map** — Not a Venn diagram. A written account, in your own words, of what consistently makes you feel most alive. It draws on the ikigai page (Day 12), the practice arc, and the kintsugi and oubaitori pages. It is narrative, not diagrammatic.

**2. Your Living Philosophy** — A single page (300–500 words) that states how you want to inhabit each day. Not a mission statement. Not goals. A statement of stance: how you want to see, act, and endure. It should name at least three concepts from this course — not as labels, but woven into sentences about your actual life.

"Done" looks like this: someone who knows you reads your living philosophy and says, *"Yes — that is actually how you are at your best."* If it sounds like a generic self-help manifesto, keep revising. If it sounds like you, you're finished.
