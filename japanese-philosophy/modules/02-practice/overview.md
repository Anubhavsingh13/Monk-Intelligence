# Module 2 — Practice (Days 9–17)

> **What this module earns you:** By the end of Day 17, you will have a set of lived practices rooted in the philosophical stance of the perception arc. You will be able to apply ikigai, kaizen, shokunin, and kintsugi to your own work and relationships — not as tools to add to your life but as expressions of a way of being you've already begun.

---

## What This Module Is For

The perception arc changed how you see. This module asks: now that you see differently, how do you act?

The temptation here is to treat the practice concepts as a to-do list: do zazen daily, apply kaizen to a habit, find your ikigai. That is the self-help reading of these ideas. The philosophical reading is different: each practice *grows from* the perception arc. Zazen grows from shoshin. Ikigai grows from ichi-go ichi-e. Kaizen grows from wabi-sabi. If you approach the practice concepts without the perception arc underneath them, they become techniques. With the perception arc, they become philosophy in action.

---

## The Inner Logic of This Module

The practice arc has three layers:

**The foundation (Days 10–11):** Zazen and mushin. Before naming what animates you or how you want to grow, you need to know what it feels like to be present and unobstructed. These two days are the experiential ground for everything that follows.

**The direction (Days 12–15):** Ikigai, kaizen, shu-ha-ri, shokunin. With the ground in place, you can ask: what am I moving toward, and how? These four concepts map the territory of purposeful, patient, craftsman-quality engagement with daily life.

**The obstacle (Days 16–17):** Kintsugi and oubaitori. Even with clarity of direction, life breaks things and comparison corrodes confidence. These two days turn the obstacles into resources.

## A Note on Zazen (Day 10)

Day 10 includes a practical sitting guide. You do not need a cushion, a special room, or any prior meditation experience. You need a chair, fifteen minutes, and the willingness to stay with what arises. The instruction is simple enough to follow immediately; the practice deepens over a lifetime. Do not wait until you feel "ready" — that is the opposite of shoshin.
