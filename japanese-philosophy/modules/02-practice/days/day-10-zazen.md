# Day 10 — Zazen: The Sitting Practice

> **Today's one idea:** Zazen is not relaxation, visualisation, or emptying your mind — it is the practice of being exactly where you are, without adding anything.
> **Reading time:** ~40 min (includes a 10-min sitting) · **Prereqs:** [Day 8](../../01-perception/days/day-08-shoshin.md), [Day 9](day-09-rest-synthesize.md)
> **Primary source for today:** Suzuki, *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind*, Part One ("Right Practice"), pp. 1–52

---

## The Hook

Sit down right now in whatever chair you're in. Put down this page for thirty seconds. Do nothing.

Notice what happens.

Within a few seconds — perhaps immediately — your mind begins to move. A thought about something you need to do. A memory that surfaces for no apparent reason. A small anxiety. A planning rehearsal for a conversation that hasn't happened. A fragment of music. A physical sensation you suddenly notice. A judgement about how you're doing at sitting still.

This is your mind's ordinary condition. It has been like this all day — you just haven't been watching it.

Zazen does not stop this movement. It teaches you to watch it without being swept away by it. And in the watching, something shifts.

---

## Building the Intuition

Imagine you are sitting by a river.

Leaves, twigs, and debris float downstream. You watch them pass. You do not jump in to grab them. You do not try to stop the river. You simply sit and watch, and the water moves past you.

Now: your thoughts are the leaves. Your mind is the river. *You* — the one watching — are the person on the bank.

Zazen is the practice of learning to sit on the bank rather than being swept downstream.

The reason this is difficult is that we are not used to distinguishing between the river and the watcher. Most of the time, we are *in* the river — pulled along by each thought as if it were an urgent summons, an identity, a fact about the world. A thought arises — "I'm terrible at this" — and we believe it, argue with it, expand it. Another thought — "I should call so-and-so" — and we're already planning the call.

Zazen does not cure this. It reveals it. And in the revealing, something becomes possible that wasn't before: the recognition that you are not the same as your thoughts. You are the one who can watch them.

This is the philosophical point of zazen — not relaxation, not stress reduction (though both may occur), but the *direct experience* of being a watcher rather than a passenger. Once you have had this experience even once, the entire perception arc you've built becomes not merely understood but *embodied*.

## The Formal Picture

**Zazen (座禅):**
- *Za* (座): to sit
- *Zen* (禅): from Sanskrit *dhyāna*, meaning meditative absorption — which entered Chinese as *chán* and Japanese as *zen*

Zazen is the central practice of the Zen schools of Buddhism. It is not an auxiliary practice or a warm-up; in the Soto Zen tradition (founded in Japan by Dōgen, 1200–1253), *zazen* is the practice — not a means to enlightenment but the direct expression of it. Sitting is not preparation for being present. Sitting *is* being present.

---

## How to Sit: The Complete Instructions

You need nothing special. A chair, a quiet room, fifteen minutes.

### Posture

**If sitting on the floor:** Cross your legs in any comfortable position. A cushion (*zafu*) under your sit-bones is helpful but not required. If you use the full or half-lotus position, ensure it is actually comfortable — forcing an uncomfortable posture will fill your sitting with physical struggle and is not the point.

**If sitting in a chair:** Sit near the front edge — not leaning against the back. Feet flat on the floor. The chair supports your weight but you are not collapsed into it.

**For all positions:**
- Spine erect but not rigid. Imagine a thread pulling gently upward from the crown of your head. The spine stacks — not military straight, but naturally elongated.
- Chin slightly tucked — so the back of the neck is long, not compressed.
- Shoulders relaxed and dropped. Not hunched, not held back — simply natural.
- Hands: rest them in your lap, left hand resting in right hand, both palms facing upward, thumbs lightly touching to form an oval. This is the *hōkaijō-in* (cosmic mudra). It is not mandatory — hands resting gently on thighs also works. Notice that the mudra, if you use it, gives you information: when the thumbs press together you're tense; when they collapse apart you're drowsy.
- Eyes: half-open, cast downward at roughly a 45-degree angle. Not focused on anything. This is counter-intuitive — most meditation traditions suggest closing the eyes. Zen keeps the eyes open to stay connected to the present environment, not withdrawn into a private inner world. Soft focus, like looking at nothing specific.
- Mouth: closed, jaw relaxed. Tongue rests lightly on the upper palate just behind the teeth. Breathe through the nose.

### The Breath

Let it be natural. Do not control it. If it helps to have a simple anchor in the early sessions, you can count breaths: *inhale* (one), *exhale* (two), up to ten, then begin again. When you lose count — and you will — simply return to one. Without judgement. Without drama.

You are not trying to breathe in a special way. You are simply allowing the breath to be the object of attention when everything else drifts away.

### The Practice

You will have thoughts. Many thoughts. This is not a failure; it is the practice revealing itself. The instruction is not "don't think." It is: when you notice you've been following a thought, *return*. Return to the posture. Return to the breath. Return to the room.

Each return is the practice. You are not failing when you follow a thought. You are practicing when you notice it and return.

Suzuki: "The goal of practice is always to keep your beginner's mind."

### Duration

Begin with ten to fifteen minutes. Set a gentle timer so you are not watching the clock. Over weeks, you might extend to twenty or thirty minutes — but ten minutes of genuine sitting is worth more than forty minutes of mental argument with yourself about sitting.

Morning, before the day's activity fills your mind, is the traditional time. But any time is the right time.

## The Philosophy Inside the Practice

Zazen is not merely a technique. It is the embodied form of everything the perception arc has built:

- **Mono no aware:** Thoughts arise and pass. You watch them pass without clinging. This is impermanence made visceral.
- **Ma:** The space between thoughts is itself the practice. You are cultivating the interval.
- **Shoshin:** You approach each breath as if you have never breathed before. Each sitting is a first sitting.
- **Ichi-go ichi-e:** This specific sitting — this ten minutes, this breath, this return from this particular drift — will never happen again.

Dōgen wrote: *Shinjin datsuraku* — "body and mind fallen away." Not destroyed; *fallen away* as an obstacle. When zazen is working, even briefly, the usual self-commentary goes quiet. What remains is simply: sitting, breathing, present.

---

## Where It Breaks / What It Is Not

**Zazen is not emptying your mind.** This is the most common misconception, and it discourages countless beginners. The mind does not empty. The practice is not to make it empty. The practice is to watch it fill and empty without being carried away. If you sit for fifteen minutes and have a thousand thoughts, you have not failed. You have had a thousand opportunities to practice returning.

**It is not relaxation, though relaxation may occur.** Zazen is alert, upright, awake. If you are falling asleep, you have entered a different state — drowsiness, not zazen. The eyes-open instruction is partly for this reason.

**It is not thinking about the concepts in this course.** Sitting time is not philosophy study. When concepts arise — "is this ichi-go ichi-e?" — notice them and return. The thinking can happen before and after. During zazen, the instruction is simple: sit.

**Progress is non-linear.** A difficult sit — restless, distracted, uncomfortable — is not a regression. It is often the most instructive. The mind is revealing itself honestly. The quiet, still sit is also not a "success" — it is just a different quality of the same practice.

## Try It Yourself

**The practice is the exercise today.**

Set a timer for 10 minutes. Sit in the posture described above. Follow the instructions. When the timer sounds, sit for thirty more seconds without moving.

Afterward, write three sentences: what arose, what returning felt like, and one thing you noticed about your mind that surprised you.

Do this again tomorrow morning, before you read Day 11. Not because you must — because Day 11 will land differently if you have sat twice rather than once.

<details>
<summary>If sitting feels impossible or painful</summary>
Physical discomfort is common in the first sessions. A few adjustments: use a firm cushion under the sit-bones; try a chair with feet flat on the floor; if the legs fall asleep, shift slightly and continue. Genuine pain (not just discomfort or restlessness) is a signal to adjust your posture, not to push through. The posture should be comfortable enough to forget — not a source of suffering that competes with the practice.
</details>

## Connect It Back

Yesterday the perception arc settled. Today you stepped through the door into practice — not by learning a new concept but by *doing* something. Tomorrow, mushin will show you the action-state that zazen opens into: the quality of doing-without-interference that the river-and-bank metaphor is pointing toward. The connection between today and tomorrow is direct: you cannot fully understand mushin without having sat, even once.

**The question you should now be able to answer:** What is the difference between thinking *about* the breath and returning *to* the breath? Why does zazen describe this distinction as the whole practice?

## Suggested Readings for Today

**Required if you have 15 extra minutes:**
Suzuki, Shunryu. *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind.* Shambhala, 1970. — Read Part One ("Right Practice"), pp. 1–52. Suzuki covers posture, breathing, and the nature of the mind in sitting practice. His instructions are simple, precise, and carry the same quality of attention they are describing. Read this *after* your first sit today.

**If you want the deep version:**
- Suzuki, *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind* — Part Three ("Right Understanding"), pp. 111–138. The philosophical grounding of the practice: what Dōgen meant by *shinjin datsuraku*, and why sitting is not preparation for something else but the thing itself.
- Herrigel, Eugen. *Zen in the Art of Archery.* Pantheon, 1953. — Chapters One and Two. Herrigel's account of learning to release an arrow "correctly" — without *him* releasing it — is the most vivid narrative account of what zazen is building toward. Read it as a story about the mind, not about archery.
