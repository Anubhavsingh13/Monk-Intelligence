# Glossary — Japanese Philosophy for Better Living

> Alphabetical. Each term has a plain-English definition, a formal definition, and the day it first appears. When a term feels slippery while reading, come here first.

---

## A

**Aware (哀れ)**
*Plain English:* A sensitivity or attunement to things — the capacity to be moved by reality as it is, not as you wish it were. Neither sadness nor sentimentality, but a kind of open receptivity.
*Formal:* A Japanese aesthetic and ethical term denoting pathos, poignancy, and empathetic resonance with the transient nature of things. One of the root concepts underlying mono no aware.
*Introduced:* Day 1

---

## G

**Gaman (我慢)**
*Plain English:* Enduring difficulty with quiet dignity, without drama or complaint. Not suppressing your feelings — acknowledging difficulty fully while continuing with composure.
*Formal:* From *ga* (我, self/ego) and *man* (慢, endurance/tolerance). A Buddhist-rooted concept of patient self-mastery through adversity. Distinct from passive resignation (*akirame*) and Western stoic suppression.
*Introduced:* Day 19

---

## I

**Ichi-go Ichi-e (一期一会)**
*Plain English:* "One time, one meeting." The recognition that every encounter is unrepeatable — this exact configuration of people, circumstances, and moment has never existed before and never will again.
*Formal:* *Ichi-go* (一期, one lifetime) + *ichi-e* (一会, one meeting). A concept rooted in the Japanese tea ceremony tradition, most closely associated with tea master Sen no Rikyū (1522–1591). Each gathering is treated as a once-in-a-lifetime event.
*Introduced:* Day 7

**Ikigai (生き甲斐)**
*Plain English:* The small, daily sense of "it is good to be alive right now." Not a grand life purpose but a present-tense feeling of aliveness — something as small as a morning coffee or a conversation with a friend can be ikigai.
*Formal:* From *iki* (生き, life/living) and *gai* (甲斐, worth/result/fruit). A Japanese concept denoting that which makes one feel that life is worth living. Documented in longevity research in Okinawa; distinct from the Western Venn-diagram interpretation.
*Introduced:* Day 12

---

## K

**Kaizen (改善)**
*Plain English:* Continuous, small, patient improvement. The philosophy that change is not an event but an ongoing process — becoming slightly more yourself each day rather than trying to transform into a different person.
*Formal:* From *kai* (改, change/reform) and *zen* (善, good). Originally a manufacturing philosophy (Toyota Production System); philosophically rooted in Japanese cultural attitudes toward craft and gradual mastery.
*Introduced:* Day 13

**Kintsugi (金継ぎ)**
*Plain English:* The art of repairing broken ceramics with gold — and the philosophy that breakage is part of an object's (and a person's) history, to be honoured rather than hidden.
*Formal:* From *kin* (金, gold) and *tsugi* (継ぎ, joinery/repair). A Japanese ceramic repair technique using urushi lacquer mixed with gold, silver, or platinum powder. Philosophically: wabi-sabi applied to damage and loss.
*Introduced:* Day 16

**Kokoro (心)**
*Plain English:* The heart-mind — the Japanese understanding that thinking and feeling are not separate faculties but expressions of one whole person attending to the world.
*Formal:* The character 心 depicts a heart and is used in Japanese to refer to mind, heart, spirit, and intention collectively. Cannot be translated as simply "mind" or simply "heart" without losing meaning. Used in compounds: *kokoro-gake* (devotion/attention), *kokoro-mochi* (feeling/mood), *kokoro-zashi* (aspiration).
*Introduced:* Day 2

---

## M

**Ma (間)**
*Plain English:* The interval — the pause, gap, or empty space that gives meaning to what surrounds it. Not absence but active presence: the silence between notes that makes the music possible.
*Formal:* The character 間 combines "gate" (門) and "sun/moon" (日) — light coming through a gate: a deliberate, meaningful interval. Used in time (pause), space (gap), and relationship (considered distance). A foundational Japanese aesthetic and architectural concept.
*Introduced:* Day 5

**Mono no Aware (物の哀れ)**
*Plain English:* The bittersweet ache of impermanence — the way beauty is sharpened, not cancelled, by knowing it will end. The feeling of watching cherry blossoms fall.
*Formal:* *Mono* (物, things/phenomena) + *no* (の, possessive) + *aware* (哀れ, pathos/poignancy). Articulated by Japanese literary scholar Motoori Norinaga (1730–1801) in his reading of *The Tale of Genji*. The central concept of Japanese aesthetic sensitivity.
*Introduced:* Day 3

**Mottainai (もったいない)**
*Plain English:* "What a waste" — a feeling of regret at squandering something of inherent value. Applies not just to objects but to attention, time, and aliveness.
*Formal:* An untranslatable Japanese exclamation expressing regret over waste. Originally a Buddhist term conveying the intrinsic value (*tai*) of all things. Popularised globally by Kenyan environmental activist Wangari Maathai as a framework for reduce, reuse, recycle, and respect.
*Introduced:* Day 18

**Mujo (無常)**
*Plain English:* Impermanence — the basic fact that everything changes and nothing is fixed. Not a tragedy but the fundamental fabric of reality.
*Formal:* From *mu* (無, not/without) and *jo* (常, constant/permanent). A central Buddhist concept (*anicca* in Pali) absorbed and culturally deepened in Japan over a thousand years. The ground from which mono no aware, wabi-sabi, and ichi-go ichi-e all grow.
*Introduced:* Day 1

**Mushin (無心)**
*Plain English:* No-mind — the state of acting without ego interference, without overthinking, without the narrator in your head commenting on what you're doing. Total, unobstructed engagement.
*Formal:* From *mu* (無, not/without) and *shin/kokoro* (心, heart-mind). Not the absence of thought but the absence of *clinging* to thought. From Zen and martial arts traditions; articulated by Zen monk Takuan Soho (1573–1645).
*Introduced:* Day 11

---

## O

**Oubaitori (おうばいとり)**
*Plain English:* The recognition that each person blooms in their own time and their own way — comparison with others is a category error, like comparing a cherry tree to a plum tree for blooming at different times.
*Formal:* A compound formed from the first characters of four Japanese spring flowering trees: *ou* (桜, cherry), *bai* (梅, plum), *tou* (桃, peach), *ri* (李, apricot). Each blooms at its own time; none is early or late.
*Introduced:* Day 17

---

## S

**Sabi (寂)**
*Plain English:* The beauty that comes with age, use, and the patina of time. The quality of something that has been worn by living.
*Formal:* Originally meaning "loneliness" or "chill"; evolved to denote the beauty of natural ageing, weathering, and the marks of time. One of the two roots of wabi-sabi; associated with the aesthetics of tea ceremony under Sen no Rikyū.
*Introduced:* Day 4

**Shizen (自然)**
*Plain English:* Naturalness — the quality of something being fully and unconstrainedly what it is. Not crude, but uncontrived. The Japanese aesthetic ideal of authenticity without performance.
*Formal:* Literally "self-so" (*ji*: self, *zen*: so/thus). Used both as the Japanese word for "nature" and as an aesthetic principle: the quality of acting from one's own nature without forcing or affectation.
*Introduced:* Day 1

**Shoshin (初心)**
*Plain English:* Beginner's mind — the quality of approaching something with genuine openness, curiosity, and lack of fixed assumptions, even when you have experience with it.
*Formal:* From *sho* (初, beginning/first) and *shin* (心, kokoro/heart-mind). A Zen concept; most famously articulated for Western readers by Shunryu Suzuki: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few."
*Introduced:* Day 8

**Shokunin (職人)**
*Plain English:* A craftsperson who has devoted themselves to their work as a practice — not merely skilled, but someone whose work is an expression of their care for others and their commitment to doing the thing fully.
*Formal:* From *shoku* (職, occupation/calling) and *nin* (人, person). Not just "craftsman" but a person whose vocation defines their relationship with the world. The shokunin spirit applies to any work done with full attention and care.
*Introduced:* Day 15

**Shu-Ha-Ri (守破離)**
*Plain English:* The three stages of mastery: first, follow the rules completely and faithfully (shu); then, begin to understand and question them (ha); finally, transcend them because the rules have become part of you (ri).
*Formal:* *Shu* (守, protect/follow), *ha* (破, break/detach), *ri* (離, separate/transcend). From Japanese martial arts and tea ceremony traditions. A framework for the full arc of learning in any discipline.
*Introduced:* Day 14

---

## W

**Wabi (侘)**
*Plain English:* Rustic simplicity — finding richness in less, in the unpolished, in the deliberately understated.
*Formal:* Originally meaning poverty, loneliness, or desolation; over centuries transmuted into a positive aesthetic ideal of humble, rustic simplicity. Most closely associated with Sen no Rikyū's transformation of the tea ceremony.
*Introduced:* Day 4

**Wabi-Sabi (侘寂)**
*Plain English:* The beauty of things that are imperfect, incomplete, and impermanent — an acceptance that these qualities are not flaws to be corrected but truths to be inhabited.
*Formal:* The compound of wabi (rustic simplicity) and sabi (beauty of age and wear). A Japanese aesthetic philosophy that celebrates transience and imperfection as the ground of beauty. Emerged from Zen Buddhism and tea ceremony culture; most fully elaborated by Leonard Koren (1994).
*Introduced:* Day 4

---

## Y

**Yūgen (幽玄)**
*Plain English:* A profound, mysterious sense of the beauty of the universe — the feeling you get watching fog move over a mountain, or a lone boat disappear into the evening sea. Too deep to explain; only pointing at it is possible.
*Formal:* From *yū* (幽, dim/faint/deep) and *gen* (玄, dark/mysterious/profound). Originally a Chinese aesthetic concept; imported and deepened in Japanese Noh theatre by playwright Zeami Motokiyo (1363–1443). Represents the highest layer of the Japanese aesthetic — the beauty that transcends explanation.
*Introduced:* Day 6

---

## Z

**Zazen (座禅)**
*Plain English:* Sitting meditation — the central practice of Zen Buddhism. Not relaxation or visualisation, but the practice of being exactly where you are, without adding anything.
*Formal:* From *za* (座, to sit) and *zen* (禅, from Sanskrit *dhyana*, meditative absorption). The foundational practice of the Soto and Rinzai Zen schools. The posture, breath, and orientation of attention all serve the same purpose: direct encounter with present experience.
*Introduced:* Day 10
