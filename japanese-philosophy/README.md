# Japanese Philosophy for Better Living

> **The promise:** By the end of this course, you will be able to identify the opportunities for better living already present in your daily life — and align your attention, choices, and values with them using twenty-one days of Japan's deepest philosophical traditions.

---

## Who This Course Is For

This course is for the curious adult who has encountered fragments — ikigai on a podcast, wabi-sabi in a design article, kaizen in a business book — and suspects there is something deeper and more coherent underneath. You do not need a background in philosophy, Japanese culture, or meditation. You need curiosity and thirty to forty-five minutes of focused attention each day.

The course targets **L1 (Practitioner)** depth: you will use these ideas to reason about your own life, see your circumstances differently, and make wiser choices. You will not read primary Japanese texts or write academic analysis — but you will understand these ideas well enough to recognise when they apply and to explain them clearly to someone else.

---

## The Arc at a Glance

On Day 1, you arrive with fragments. By Day 8, something has shifted: you see that wabi-sabi, ikigai, ma, and the rest are not separate tools but facets of a single Japanese *relationship with reality* — one that moves *with* impermanence, incompleteness, and ordinariness rather than fighting them. The first eight days are entirely about perception. They do not ask you to change anything yet. They ask you to notice.

From Day 10, the course turns toward practice — but practice that grows directly from the perception built in the first arc. Ikigai is not a Venn diagram; it is what you already feel on your best mornings. Kaizen is not a productivity system; it is what happens when you stop demanding transformation and allow becoming. By Day 21, you are not completing a template — you are writing a living document: a personal philosophy assembled from ancient ideas, fitted to the specific texture of your actual life.

## How to Use This Course

- **Rhythm:** One page per day, 30–45 minutes of focused reading. Do not skip ahead — each page assumes the ones before it.
- **Exercises:** The *Try it yourself* section on each page is not optional decoration. Even at L1 depth, doing the exercises is what moves these ideas from intellectual knowledge to usable understanding. Budget 10 extra minutes on exercise days.
- **Bibliography:** When you finish a page and want to go deeper immediately, check the *Suggested readings* section at the bottom of that page — it points to a specific chapter or timestamp. Open [`bibliography.md`](bibliography.md) only when you want the full annotated shelf.
- **Rest and synthesize days** (Days 9 and 20): These are not days off. They are when the ideas settle. Do not skip them. Use them to re-read your notes, re-derive connections, and sit with what has become clear.
- **If life intervenes:** Missing a day is fine. Do not try to catch up by reading two pages in one sitting — the spacing is part of the learning. Simply resume the next day.
- **The glossary:** When a term feels slippery, check [`glossary.md`](glossary.md) — every term is defined in plain English and formal terms, with the day it first appeared.

## The Course Shelf

The five most essential sources for this course:

1. **Koren, Leonard. *Wabi-Sabi for Artists, Designers, Poets & Philosophers.*** Stone Bridge Press, 1994. — The philosophical spine for the entire perception arc (Days 1–8).
2. **Suzuki, Shunryu. *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind.*** Shambhala, 1970. — Primary source for Shoshin (Day 8) and the meditation arc (Days 10–11).
3. **Tanizaki, Jun'ichirō. *In Praise of Shadows.*** Leete's Island Books, 1977. — The most beautiful sustained meditation on ma, wabi-sabi, and the Japanese aesthetic.
4. **García, Héctor & Miralles, Francesc. *Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life.*** Hutchinson, 2017. — Entry point for Day 12; read it alongside the page, critically.
5. **Okakura, Kakuzō. *The Book of Tea.*** Fox, Duffield & Co., 1906 (public domain). — Original articulation of ichi-go ichi-e and the shokunin spirit.

Full annotated shelf: [`bibliography.md`](bibliography.md).

## The Capstone

On Day 21, you will produce two things: a personal *ikigai map* — not the Venn diagram, but a narrative account of what makes you feel most alive, written after twenty days of seeing your life through Japanese eyes — and a one-page *living philosophy*, a brief document that states how you want to inhabit each day. "Done" looks like this: someone who knows you reads your living philosophy and says, "Yes — that is actually how you are at your best."
