# Bibliography — Japanese Philosophy for Better Living

## Foundational Books

### 1. Koren, Leonard. *Wabi-Sabi for Artists, Designers, Poets & Philosophers.*
Stone Bridge Press, 1994. Revised edition: 2008. 96 pages.

The definitive short text on wabi-sabi and the philosophical spine of the perception arc. Koren, an American designer who spent years studying Japanese aesthetics, compressed the essential metaphysics of wabi-sabi into fewer than a hundred pages without sacrificing rigour. The book is divided into three parts: a cultural-historical account of how wabi and sabi merged, a metaphysical framework ("The Wabi-Sabi Universe"), and a set of practical aesthetic principles. Where it is hardest: the metaphysical section uses language that hovers between philosophy and poetry — stay with it; the payoff comes in the practical section. **Relies on it most:** Days 3, 4, and the perception arc generally.

---

### 2. Tanizaki, Jun'ichirō. *In Praise of Shadows (陰翳礼讃).*
Originally published in Japanese, 1933. English translation by Thomas J. Harper & Edward G. Seidensticker. Leete's Island Books, 1977. 48 pages. Multiple paperback editions widely available.

A sustained, beautiful meditation on the Japanese aesthetic — specifically on how Japan's relationship with shadow, dimness, and imperfection differs fundamentally from the Western pursuit of brightness and clarity. Tanizaki, one of Japan's great novelists, wrote this as an essay comparing traditional Japanese architecture, lacquerware, food, and theatre with their Western counterparts. The entire essay is a performance of the contrast this course introduces on Day 1. Read it whole — it is only 48 pages and reads in a single sitting. Where it is hardest: some passages are culturally specific to 1930s Japan; read them for their aesthetic argument, not as social commentary. **Relies on it most:** Days 1, 4, and 5.

---

### 3. Suzuki, Shunryu. *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind: Informal Talks on Zen Meditation and Practice.*
Shambhala Publications, 1970. 138 pages. Edited by Trudy Dixon.

The foundational English-language text on Zen practice and the concept of shoshin (beginner's mind). Suzuki Roshi was a Japanese Zen monk who founded the San Francisco Zen Center; this book is a collection of his informal talks. It is not a how-to manual — it is a sustained transmission of the Zen stance toward practice, mind, and daily life. Every sentence is short and plain, but the meaning accumulates over re-reading. Part One ("Right Practice") covers the physical aspects of zazen; Part Two ("Right Attitude") covers shoshin and the relationship with one's own mind; Part Three ("Right Understanding") covers the philosophical framework. Where it is hardest: the oracular, paradoxical style is deliberate — do not try to resolve contradictions immediately. **Relies on it most:** Days 8, 10, and 11.

---

### 4. García, Héctor & Miralles, Francesc. *Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life.*
Hutchinson (UK) / Penguin Books (US), 2017. Translated from the 2016 Spanish original (*Ikigai: Los secretos de Japón para una vida larga y feliz*) by Heather Cleary. Approximately 194 pages.

The most widely read introduction to ikigai in English, based on fieldwork in Ogimi, Okinawa — the village with one of the highest concentrations of centenarians in the world. The book is accessible and narrative-driven. Its key limitation — important for Day 12 — is that it presents ikigai primarily through the Western Venn-diagram framing (what you love / are good at / the world needs / can be paid for), which is not how the concept appears in Japanese daily life or scholarship. The book is most valuable for its portraits of Okinawan centenarians and its chapters on flow and movement. Read it critically alongside Day 12, which corrects the framing. **Relies on it most:** Day 12. Also referenced in Days 13 and 18.

### 5. Masuno, Shunmyo. *The Art of Simple Living: 100 Daily Practices from a Zen Buddhist Monk for a Lifetime of Calm and Joy.*
Penguin Life, 2019. Translated from Japanese. **[VERIFY specific translator and exact page count.]**

Written by a practicing Zen Buddhist head monk and internationally recognised garden designer. The book presents 100 brief practices drawn from Zen philosophy — each is one to two pages, direct and practical without being prescriptive. The practices map cleanly onto the concepts in the practice arc: simplicity (wabi-sabi), small improvements (kaizen), presence (ichi-go ichi-e), accepting difficulty (gaman). Less theoretical than the other books on this shelf, more immediately actionable. Where it is hardest: the brevity can make individual practices feel thin; pair each one with the relevant day page to understand the philosophical root. **Relies on it most:** Days 13, 15, 18, and 19.

## Primary Texts

### 6. Okakura, Kakuzō. *The Book of Tea.*
Fox, Duffield & Company, 1906. Now in the public domain; available free in multiple online editions. Approximately 70 pages.

Written in English by a Japanese art scholar for a Western audience, this is the original articulation of the Japanese tea ceremony as a complete philosophy of living — covering ichi-go ichi-e, the shokunin spirit, wabi-sabi, and ma in their natural context. Chapters Three ("Taoism and Zennism") and Five ("Art Appreciation") are the most directly relevant. The prose is elegant and occasionally archaic; read slowly. The tea ceremony, as Okakura describes it, is not a ritual but a complete philosophy of presence enacted in time. **Relies on it most:** Days 7 and 15.

---

### 7. Soho, Takuan. *The Unfettered Mind: Writings of the Zen Master to the Sword Master.*
Translated by William Scott Wilson. Shambhala Publications, 1986. Originally written c. 1632. 104 pages.

Three letters written by the Zen monk Takuan Soho to the swordsman Yagyu Munenori. The central subject is mushin — acting without the mind getting in its own way. Takuan's description of the "abiding place of the mind" and the difference between a mind that stops and a mind that flows is the clearest primary-source account of mushin available in English. Chapter One ("The Mysterious Record of Immovable Wisdom") is the essential text for Day 11. Dense but short; re-read the key passages after Day 11. **Relies on it most:** Day 11.

---

### 8. Musashi, Miyamoto. *The Book of Five Rings (五輪書).*
Written c. 1645. English translation recommended: translated by Thomas Cleary, Shambhala Publications, 2003. 80 pages.

Musashi's strategic treatise, written at the end of his life, is not a martial-arts manual. It is a philosophy of mastery through direct perception — seeing things exactly as they are, without assumption or ornamentation. Relevant primarily to shu-ha-ri (Day 14) and the shokunin spirit (Day 15). The "Earth" and "Wind" scrolls are the most philosophically dense. Where it is hardest: the martial context can obscure the philosophical content; read it as a meditation on mastery and perception, not tactics. **Relies on it most:** Days 14 and 15.

## Academic Reference

### 9. Heisig, James W., Kasulis, Thomas P. & Maraldo, John C., eds. *Japanese Philosophy: A Sourcebook.*
University of Hawai'i Press, 2011. 1,279 pages.

The definitive academic anthology of Japanese philosophy from the Nara period (8th century) to the present. Contains primary-source excerpts from Zen thinkers, Kyoto School philosophers (including Nishida Kitaro and Nishitani Keiji), aestheticians, and ethicists, all with scholarly introductions. This is not daily reading — it is the reference shelf you open when a day page sparks a question that deserves a deeper, scholarly answer. Part Two ("Kamakura Buddhism") and Part Four ("The Modern Period") are most relevant to this course. **Relies on it most:** Background reference for Days 1, 6, and the capstone.

## Essays

### 10. Keene, Donald. "Japanese Aesthetics."
*Philosophy East and West*, Vol. 19, No. 3, July 1969, pp. 293–306. University of Hawai'i Press. **[VERIFY DOI and exact availability — search via JSTOR or Google Scholar using title and author.]**

A tight, accessible academic essay by one of the great Western scholars of Japanese literature. Keene names and distinguishes four core Japanese aesthetic principles — suggestion, irregularity, simplicity, and perishability — and shows how they differ structurally from Western aesthetics. This essay is the clearest scholarly articulation of the pattern underlying Days 1–6. At 13 pages, it is a single-sitting read. **Relies on it most:** Days 1–4 as background framing.

## Optional Deeper Dives

### 13. Herrigel, Eugen. *Zen in the Art of Archery.*
Translated by R.F.C. Hull. Pantheon Books, 1953. German original (*Zen in der Kunst des Bogenschiessens*) published 1948. 90 pages.

A German philosophy professor's account of studying kyudo (Japanese archery) in Japan under master Awa Kenzo. The central question — how do you release an arrow without "you" releasing it? — is the best narrative account of mushin available to Western readers. To be read critically: later scholarship has questioned some of Herrigel's interpretations and suggested translation difficulties affected his understanding. Read it as a vivid phenomenological account, not as doctrine. **Relevant to:** Day 11 and as an optional companion to zazen (Day 10).

---

### 14. Li, Qing. *Forest Bathing: How Trees Can Help You Find Health and Happiness.*
Viking, 2018.

A Japanese doctor and researcher's account of shinrin-yoku (forest bathing) — the practice of spending time in forests for health and psychological well-being. Part personal narrative, part scientific summary. Relevant as a somatic extension of ma (Day 5) and mottainai (Day 18). Not central to the course but a beautiful practical application if the perception arc resonates. **Relevant to:** Days 5, 18, and the capstone as a practice pointer.

---

### 15. Nishida, Kitaro. *An Inquiry into the Good (善の研究).*
Translated by Masao Abe and Christopher Ives. Yale University Press, 1990. Originally published 1911.

The foundational text of the Kyoto School — Japan's first major modern philosophical movement. Nishida's concept of "pure experience" (junsui keiken) — experience before the subject-object split — underlies much of what this course treats at an intuitive level. Not required reading, but for anyone who finishes the course and wants the philosophical architecture, Chapter One is the entry point. **Relevant to:** Day 21 (capstone, for readers who want to go further).
