# Module 5 — Strategic Life & Career
*Days 56–65 · Applying the full toolkit to your own decisions*

---

## What This Module Earns You

By the end of Day 65 you will have applied the full decision-science and game-theory toolkit to the most consequential domain: your own life. You will be able to:

- Frame career moves as sequential games with option value, commitment costs, and reputation effects.
- Construct a portfolio of life bets with uncorrelated payoffs, rather than a single concentrated path.
- Design commitment devices that bind your future self to your current values.
- Negotiate — salary, partnerships, scope — using formal bargaining theory and signalling.
- Identify the structural network positions that create and capture the most value.
- Build lasting decision hygiene habits: pre-mortems, reference class forecasting, decision journals.
- Forecast calibrated probabilities and know how to improve your calibration over time.
- Apply a unified three-layer framework (decision theory + game theory + behavioural economics) to any hard decision.

---

## The Story of This Module

The hardest problems in this course are not the formal ones. They are the ones where you are both the analyst and the subject — where your own biases are the obstacle to clear reasoning, where you have no clean payoff matrix, and where the other "players" are your future self, your colleagues, and a labour market that does not hold still.

This module does not introduce new theory. Every concept here was earned in Modules 1–4. What is new is the *application target*: your own career and life. The module works by connecting each theoretical tool to a class of real decisions — career moves as sequential games, life bets as portfolios, negotiations as bargaining problems, and social networks as coalition-formation games.

The module ends with the capstone (Day 65): two 2-page decision memos. One about an AI feature. One about your own life. They are graded by a single question: *did writing this memo help you decide something you would have otherwise decided worse?*
