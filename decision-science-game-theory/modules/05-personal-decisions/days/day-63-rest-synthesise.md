## The Seven Personal Decision Principles

One transferable principle per day. These are not summaries — they are rules designed to be invoked under pressure, without consulting notes.

**P1 — Build reputation as a long-run investment.**
Your career is not a series of independent transactions. It is a repeated game with an overlapping cast of players who remember what you did last time. Cooperation can be sustained in indefinitely repeated games if the discount factor δ is high enough and players care about the future. The implication: signal quality consistently over time, absorb short-term costs to preserve the cooperative equilibrium, and invest in being the kind of player whose reputation precedes you in new rooms. Reputation compounds. Gaming reputation depletes it.

**P2 — Bridge structural holes — be the connector.**
Most networks cluster into dense, redundant groups. The nodes with the highest strategic value are not those with the most connections but those that bridge otherwise disconnected clusters. If you are the only person who knows both the engineers and the clients, both the investors and the researchers, both the policy world and the technical world — you are a structural hole broker. This is a durable source of influence that does not require formal authority. Audit your network for holes you could bridge this month.

**P3 — Design for your actual future self, not your idealised one.**
Present-bias (β < 1 in the β-δ model) means your future self will discount the future more steeply than your current self intends. Commitment devices work by removing the choice from the moment of temptation. The design principle: assume your future self will be tired, distracted, and motivated differently than you are right now, then build the environment accordingly. Ulysses tied himself to the mast. You use automatic transfers, locked accounts, and accountability contracts. The best commitment device is the one that costs nothing when you follow through and everything when you defect.

**P4 — Improve your BATNA before the negotiation starts.**
The Nash bargaining solution is determined by disagreement points — what each party gets if talks break down. Your share of the surplus is proportional to how much your outside option has improved relative to your counterpart's. The practical implication is decisive: the most productive negotiation preparation is not studying tactics. It is improving your alternatives. Before any significant negotiation — salary, contract, partnership — spend 80% of your preparation time expanding your BATNA. Tactics matter at the margin. Outside options determine the centre.

**P5 — Start with the base rate, then adjust for specifics.**
Every decision involves a question of frequency: how often do projects like this succeed? How long do transitions like this take? How often do partnerships like this hold? Your inside view — the narrative of why this case is special — is usually wrong. The outside view is almost always more accurate. The procedure is simple: identify the reference class, find the base rate, anchor there, then adjust for specific features. The adjustment should be smaller than your instincts suggest. Overconfidence is the default; the base rate is the correction.

**P6 — Make probabilistic predictions and track them rigorously.**
A decision made at 80% confidence means something only if you are right about 80% of the time when you say 80%. Calibration is a skill, and like all skills it requires deliberate practice and feedback. Keep a prediction log. Assign probabilities. Score yourself monthly with the Brier score. Over time, systematic biases will emerge — you will find that you are overconfident in your own domain and underconfident in others'. Calibration practice is one of the highest-return epistemic investments available to a decision-maker, and almost no one does it.

**P7 — Hold a barbell — stable floor, asymmetric upside bets.**
Nassim Taleb's barbell strategy applied to personal decisions: hold most of your resources in low-variance, safe positions (stable income, strong relationships, liquid savings), and allocate a small fraction to high-variance, asymmetric bets (the new skill, the side project, the speculative investment). Avoid the middle — medium-variance positions carry the worst risk-reward profile because the downside is not bounded and the upside is not unbounded. The Kelly Criterion formalises this: the correct bet size is proportional to your edge. When edge is unknown, bet small. When edge is large and the downside is bounded, bet proportionally more.

**Q1 (Day 56 — Career as Repeated Game)**

Two colleagues with equal skill negotiate salary. Alice has a competing offer; Bob doesn't. Rubinstein's alternating-offer model says patience determines the split. How does it predict the outcome here, and what should Bob do before his next negotiation?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

In Rubinstein's model, the player with the higher discount factor (more patient, or more credibly able to walk away) captures a larger share of the surplus. Alice's competing offer functions as an improved disagreement point: if talks break down, she gets the competing salary. Bob's disagreement point is unemployment or staying at his current role. The model predicts Alice will receive a larger share of the available surplus — the employer will make a better offer to avoid losing her.

What should Bob do? Improve his BATNA before the next negotiation. That means: actively interview elsewhere, develop a competing offer, or build a credible outside option (consulting project, public work that makes him visible). The competing offer need not be accepted — it just needs to exist. Once Bob has an outside option, he becomes a patient negotiator, and Rubinstein's model shifts the predicted split in his favour. Tactics and preparation matter at the margin; outside options determine the centre.

</details>

**Q2 (Day 57 — Network Strategy)**

You are a software engineer embedded in a technical team. Your company's marketing team and engineering team rarely interact. You have relationships with members of both. What is your structural position, and how should you use it?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

You occupy a structural hole — you bridge two dense, internally connected clusters that have no other pathway between them. Burt's research shows that structural hole brokers have higher salaries, faster promotions, and better access to diverse information than equally skilled non-brokers.

The strategic uses of this position: (1) Information arbitrage — technical insights about feasibility can be translated into marketing language and vice versa, giving you a unique informational advantage in both groups. (2) Coordination — you can initiate joint projects that neither group would organise on their own. (3) Reputation — you become visible to senior leadership precisely because organisational friction flows through you. The risk: each side may perceive you as capturing value rather than creating it. Manage this by visibly creating mutual benefit — introduce people, translate, and attribute value generously.

</details>

**Q3 (Day 58 — Commitment Devices)**

You have decided to study one hour each evening. By 9 pm, you are consistently too tired to follow through, and you have rationalised skipping for the past two weeks. What does the β-δ model say about the problem, and what commitment device would address it?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The β-δ model separates long-run patience (δ) from present bias (β). Your long-run self wants to study (high δ — you value the future benefit). Your present self, when 9 pm arrives, discounts the immediate cost of effort too heavily relative to the future reward (β < 1). This is time-inconsistency: preferences held now conflict with preferences you will hold at the moment of action.

Commitment devices address this by binding your future self before the temptation moment arrives. Options that apply here: (1) Schedule the study block in the morning, when β is higher and willpower reserves are fuller — remove 9 pm as the decision point entirely. (2) Implementation intention: link the cue "after dinner, before any screen" to the action, removing deliberation at the moment of low β. (3) Accountability contract: commit publicly to a study partner or use a platform like Beeminder, where failing the commitment has a monetary cost. (4) Reduce friction to near zero: keep materials open and ready, so the activation energy of starting is minimal. The goal is not to fight present bias with willpower — it is to make future-you's preferred action the path of least resistance.

</details>

**Q4 (Day 59 — Negotiation)**

You are 85% confident your project will finish in 3 months. Your reference class — projects of this type and complexity — has a median completion time of 9 months, with 90% finishing within 18 months. How should you update your estimate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

This is a classic inside-view vs. outside-view conflict. Your 85% confidence in 3 months is generated by the inside view: you are reasoning from the specific features of your project, your team's capacity, the scope as currently understood. The reference class provides the outside view: how long projects like this actually take, across many instances, including all the unknown unknowns.

The procedure: anchor on the reference class median (9 months), then adjust for specific features that credibly distinguish your case. Because overconfidence is the systematic error, the adjustment should be conservative. A reasonable updated estimate might be: 50% probability of finishing within 6 months (anchored toward the base rate, with modest updating for your inside view), 85% within 12 months (tighter than the reference class 90% at 18 months, but not by much). Your original 3-month figure at 85% almost certainly reflects planning fallacy — the systematic underestimation of time, cost, and obstacles in self-generated plans. Default to the base rate.

</details>

**Q5 (Day 60 — Decision Hygiene)**

You are deciding whether to invest significant time in learning a new skill. You feel 90% confident it will be valuable. What questions should you ask to audit this confidence estimate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Decision hygiene applied to a confidence estimate involves several checks: (1) Reference class: what fraction of people who invest this time in similar skills find it valuable, defined concretely? If you cannot name the reference class, you have no outside view anchor. (2) Inside view vs. outside view: your 90% is likely generated by a narrative about why this skill matters for your specific situation. What does the base rate say? (3) EVSI check: is there information you could cheaply acquire that would substantially shift this estimate? If so, acquire it before committing. (4) Reversibility: how costly is it to stop midway? If the investment is reversible, a lower confidence threshold is appropriate. (5) Motivated reasoning check: do you want this skill to be valuable (for reasons of identity, sunk cost, social influence) independent of its actual value? Motivated reasoning inflates confidence estimates. Run a pre-mortem: what would it look like if this investment were not valuable? Can you articulate a credible scenario?

</details>

**Q6 (Day 61 — Calibration)**

Your Brier score log reveals that across 50 predictions made at 80% confidence, you were right 62 times out of 80 total predictions in that bucket (77.5%). Is this good calibration? Where is the residual error coming from?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A calibrated forecaster who says 80% should be right 80% of the time in the 80% bucket. You are right 77.5% of the time when you say 80% — this is close to well-calibrated, with slight overconfidence. The gap (2.5 percentage points) is within the noise for a sample of this size, but if it appears consistently across many buckets it indicates systematic mild overconfidence. The Brier score for each prediction is (f − o)², where f is your stated probability and o is 0 or 1. A perfect score is 0; a score of 0.25 corresponds to random 50/50 guessing. To improve: identify the domains where overconfidence is largest (usually domains of expertise, where the illusion of understanding is strongest) and apply stronger outside-view corrections there. Calibration tends to improve fastest when you get quick feedback — favour predictions in domains where outcomes are observed within weeks, not years.

</details>

**Q7 (Day 62 — Portfolio Thinking)**

You have two skills to develop: Skill A has high expected value and is highly correlated with your current role. Skill B has lower expected value but is uncorrelated with your current role. From a portfolio perspective, which should you invest more time developing?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The portfolio answer is not simply "the higher expected value skill." In a portfolio context, what matters is risk-adjusted return, which accounts for correlation. Skill A has high EV but its value is highly correlated with your current role — if your role becomes obsolete, devalued, or disrupted, Skill A depreciates simultaneously with your primary income source. You are doubling down on the same exposure.

Skill B has lower EV but is uncorrelated — it hedges your portfolio. If your primary role is disrupted, Skill B's value is unaffected or may even increase (as a route out). The diversification benefit of an uncorrelated asset is large, particularly when your human capital is already heavily concentrated in one domain. The portfolio-optimal answer: develop both, but do not under-weight Skill B simply because its standalone EV is lower. The relevant metric is the marginal reduction in portfolio variance it provides per unit of time invested. In most careers, this argues for more deliberate investment in low-correlation skills than intuition suggests.

</details>

## The Internal Audit

Set aside 20 minutes. Work through these questions with your decision journal open. There are no right answers — only honest ones.

**Q1:** What is my reputation signal in my primary domain, and does it satisfy the single-crossing condition? That is, would it be too costly for a low-quality person to imitate? If not, what signal could I send that would be credibly costly to fake?

**Q2:** Do I have any structural holes in my network I could bridge this month? Name two communities or groups you have relationships with that do not know each other. Is there a concrete introduction, collaboration, or project you could initiate?

**Q3:** Where am I most time-inconsistent? What commitment device would address it? Be specific: not "I should exercise more" but "at 7 pm on weekdays I consistently fail to start; the device is a pre-booked class with a cancellation fee."

**Q4:** In my last three negotiations — salary, contract, scope, or otherwise — what was my BATNA entering each one? Did I actively try to improve it before the negotiation began, or did I walk in with the BATNA I happened to have?

**Q5:** Looking at my prediction track record — if I kept one — am I well-calibrated or systematically overconfident? In which domains? If I do not have a track record, what does that fact tell me about my epistemic process?
