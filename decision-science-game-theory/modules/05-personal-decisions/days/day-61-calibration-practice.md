# Day 61 — Calibration Practice: Training Probabilistic Intuition

**Today's one idea:** Calibration — the alignment between your confidence levels and your actual accuracy — is a trainable skill, and the single most effective training method is making explicit probabilistic predictions, tracking them systematically, and receiving feedback on the gap between stated confidence and actual frequency.

**Prerequisites:** [Day 60 — Decision Hygiene](./day-60-decision-hygiene.md) · Day 9 — Decision Under Uncertainty · [Day 42 — Exploration-Exploitation](../../04-ai-products/days/day-42-exploration-exploitation.md) (prior/posterior update intuition)

"Psychological Strategies for Winning a Geopolitical Forecasting Tournament." *Psychological Science,* 25(5):1106–1115, 2014.

## The Hook

The Good Judgment Project was a four-year forecasting tournament funded by IARPA (the intelligence community's research arm) that began in 2011. More than 20,000 participants — academics, policy analysts, curious civilians — were asked to make probabilistic predictions about geopolitical events: elections, international crises, economic indicators. Their predictions were tracked, outcomes were recorded, and calibration scores were computed.

Most participants performed at roughly chance level. A small group — Tetlock called them "superforecasters" — consistently outperformed not only the general participant pool but also professional intelligence analysts with access to classified information.

What distinguished them was not IQ. It was not expertise in the relevant subject area. It was not access to privileged information. It was three things:

1. They tracked their predictions and received systematic feedback on the gap between stated confidence and actual accuracy.
2. They thought in probabilities rather than binary true-or-false outcomes.
3. They updated on new evidence without anchoring too hard on prior beliefs.

Calibration is a skill. It can be trained. And unlike many skills covered in this course, it has a tight, measurable feedback loop — you can observe your improvement within weeks.

## Building the Intuition

### 1. What Calibration Means Formally

A forecaster is **perfectly calibrated** if: among all predictions made with stated confidence X%, exactly X% turn out to be true. That's the full definition — it's a statement about a distribution of predictions across many different events.

This means:
- Your 70% confidence predictions should be correct 70% of the time — not 90%, not 50%.
- Your 90% confidence intervals should contain the true answer 90% of the time.
- Your "almost certain" predictions should be wrong occasionally.

Most people violate this in a systematic direction: **overconfidence.** Their 90% confidence intervals contain the true answer only 50–70% of the time. They are not lying — they genuinely feel 90% confident. The problem is that the feeling of confidence is generated by fluency of retrieval, narrative coherence, and familiarity, not by actual accuracy. These are all inside-view signals (Day 60), not outside-view signals.

Some experts exhibit the opposite bias — **underconfidence.** Asked about topics where they are genuinely knowledgeable, they hedge excessively: "It's hard to say," "There are many factors," "It could go either way." This is epistemic cowardice dressed as humility. "It might happen" is not a calibrated prediction; it gives no actionable information.

The goal is neither overconfidence nor underconfidence — it is **epistemic honesty:** your stated confidence should be a genuine representation of your uncertainty, based on evidence rather than comfort.

### 2. Fermi Estimation as Calibration Training

Before you can be calibrated about uncertain events, you need to practise the cognitive process of decomposing unknown quantities into estimable components. Fermi estimation is the ideal training ground.

**The classic example.** How many piano tuners are there in Chicago?

You don't know this directly. But you can decompose it:
- Chicago population: ~2.7 million
- Fraction of households with a piano: ~5% (rough estimate — pianos are expensive and declining)
- Pianos in Chicago: 2.7M × 0.05 ≈ 135,000
- Times tuned per year per piano: once per year on average
- Piano-tunings per year: 135,000
- Tunings per tuner per working day: a tuner can do about 4 pianos per day
- Working days per year: ~250
- Tunings per tuner per year: 4 × 250 = 1,000
- Piano tuners in Chicago: 135,000 / 1,000 ≈ 135

The actual answer is around 100–200 depending on the year and source. The Fermi estimate lands within a factor of 2 without knowing anything specific about Chicago's piano industry.

**Why this trains calibration.** Each step in the decomposition requires you to think about base rates and orders of magnitude — exactly the reference class reasoning from Day 60. You're forced to anchor to plausible numbers rather than pure intuition. And when you then state a 90% confidence interval around your estimate, you're practising the skill of converting a point estimate into an honest uncertainty range.

**The discipline:** after every Fermi estimate, do two things. State your 90% CI explicitly ("I'm 90% confident the answer is between 75 and 350 piano tuners"). Then look up the answer and record whether the true value was within your interval. Over dozens of estimates, you'll see your calibration pattern.

A well-calibrated Fermi estimator should have the true answer within their 90% CI roughly 90% of the time. If you're hitting at 60%, your intervals are too narrow — you're overconfident about your decomposition steps. Widen them.

### 3. The Four Superforecaster Practices (Tetlock & Gardner 2015)

From the Good Judgment Project data, Tetlock distilled four specific practices that distinguished superforecasters from typical participants. These are not personality traits — they're habits that can be deliberately adopted.

**Practice 1: Use reference classes first.**
Before engaging with the specific details of the question at hand, ask: what is the base rate? What do similar historical events suggest about the probability? This is the outside view before the inside view — [Day 60](./day-60-decision-hygiene.md)'s principle in action. Only after anchoring to the reference class do you adjust for the specifics.

**Practice 2: Think in probabilities, not binary outcomes.**
The question is never "will this happen?" — the question is "what probability would I assign to this happening?" This reframing forces precision. It also makes it harder to engage in vague hedging ("maybe," "possibly," "it's complicated") because the output format demands a number.

A useful forcing function: before any significant meeting or conversation about an uncertain outcome, write down your probability. "I think there's a 65% chance the client renews." "I think there's a 40% chance this feature ships before Q3." Writing the number forces you to commit and creates a record for calibration feedback.

**Practice 3: Update incrementally on new evidence.**
New information should shift your probability — up or down, by an amount proportional to the strength of the evidence and how surprising it is given your prior. This is Bayesian updating ([Day 42](../../04-ai-products/days/day-42-exploration-exploitation.md)'s Thompson Sampling connection: your belief distribution updates each time you observe an outcome).

The specific failure mode to watch: anchoring. Once you've said "70%", strong evidence should move you to perhaps 90% or down to 40% — but most people nudge to 75% or 65%, as if the update cost is proportional to how far you move. It isn't. Bayesian updating is unsentimentally proportional to the evidence.

**Practice 4: Distinguish "I'm uncertain" from "it's random."**
Uncertainty is **epistemic** — it exists because you lack information that could in principle be known. Randomness is **aleatoric** — it is irreducible even with full information.

A coin flip (with a fair coin) is aleatoric — you genuinely cannot do better than 50% even with complete information. Your uncertainty about the outcome of a long-running business negotiation is primarily epistemic — you could in principle gather information (their BATNA, their financial position, their timeline) that would sharpen your estimate.

Treating epistemic uncertainty as if it were aleatoric ("it's basically a coin flip, who knows") is a form of intellectual laziness that forecloses useful reasoning. When you're uncertain, ask whether gathering more information would reduce the uncertainty. If yes, it's epistemic — reason carefully. If no, it's aleatoric — assign probabilities and accept the irreducible variance.

### 4. The Aggregation Effect — Crowd Wisdom vs. Crowd Madness

One of the most striking results from the Good Judgment Project: groups of calibrated individuals, aggregating independently, outperformed even the best individual superforecaster. The wisdom of crowds is real — when the crowd is calibrated and independent.

But groups of uncalibrated, correlated individuals are systematically worse than individual experts. This is the information cascade problem from Day 27: when everyone reads the same news sources, updates on the same signals, and talks to the same people, aggregating their opinions doesn't add independent information. It adds correlated noise.

**The key conditions for crowd wisdom to hold:**
- Forecasters are independent (they haven't all consulted each other before predicting)
- Forecasters have genuine information (not just shared noise)
- Aggregation weights calibrated forecasters more than uncalibrated ones

**Application to your own reasoning.** Before aggregating the opinions of people around you about an uncertain outcome, ask: are these opinions truly independent? Do they come from genuinely different information sources, or did everyone update on the same article/event/conversation? The more correlated the sources, the less aggregating them helps.

Deliberately seek views from people who have different epistemic vantage points: different industries, different geographies, different political priors, different professional backgrounds. The aggregation value is proportional to the independence.

### 5. Updating vs. Anchoring

This is a tension you'll feel in practice: you have a prior belief, new evidence arrives, and you need to update. How much should you move?

**The Bayesian answer.** Your update should be proportional to the likelihood ratio — how much more probable the evidence is under your hypothesis than under its negation. If the evidence is 5× more likely under your hypothesis, and your prior odds were 1:4 (20% probability), your posterior odds are 5:4 (roughly 56% probability). A jump from 20% to 56% on a single piece of strong evidence is not overreaction. It is correct updating.

**The anchoring failure mode.** Anchoring bias causes people to update too little. Once you've said "30%", even strong evidence tends to produce updates to 35% or 40% when 60% might be correct. This is conservatism in the technical statistical sense — systematic under-updating.

**The over-updating failure mode.** Some people, correctly understanding that they should update, swing too far — treating a single piece of dramatic news as decisive when it merely shifts the prior modestly. A company's stock price falling 5% is evidence the market is updating downward, but it is weak evidence about the company's long-term fundamentals. Don't mistake noise for signal.

**The diagnostic for your update size.** When you update your probability, ask: can I articulate the size of the update? "I moved from 40% to 55% because the trial result came in positive, which is roughly 2× more likely if the drug works than if it doesn't — that corresponds to a likelihood ratio of about 2, which should move my odds from 2:3 to 4:3, so roughly 57%." If you can't do something like this articulation, your update size is arbitrary. Practise making the reasoning explicit.

### 6. Building Your Calibration Training Regimen

Calibration improves through deliberate practice with tight feedback loops. Here is a concrete training structure:

**Daily:** one Fermi estimation. Pick a number you don't know — a city's population, a country's GDP, a historical date, a physical measurement. Decompose it, state your estimate, state your 90% CI, look it up, record whether the true value was within your interval. This takes 5–10 minutes per day and builds the reference class reasoning habit as a background process.

**Weekly:** 3–5 predictions about events resolving within 4 weeks. Your work domain is ideal — feature launch dates, deal close probabilities, team velocity estimates. State explicit probabilities. Record the outcomes. Compute your running Brier score.

**Online practice with ground truth:** Metaculus, Manifold Markets, and Good Judgment Open are public forecasting platforms where you make probabilistic predictions on real events with defined resolution criteria. The platforms track your calibration across hundreds of predictions, show your calibration curve, and let you compare yourself to other forecasters. This provides the feedback loop that self-tracking cannot — someone else defines the question and the resolution criterion, removing your ability to game the data.

**Annual review:** once per year, review your prediction log from the previous year. Where were you systematically miscalibrated? The patterns you'll typically see:
- Overconfident on novel situations (new domain, unprecedented events)
- Underconfident on recurring patterns (team velocity in Q4, deal closure rates with familiar customer types)
- Better calibrated in professional domain than personal domain

Use these patterns to adjust your stated confidence levels going forward: if you've been systematically overconfident in novel situations, apply an explicit "discount factor" to your confidence — state 10 percentage points lower than your gut confidence as a standing correction until the calibration log shows improvement.

## Formal Picture

**The calibration curve.**

Plot your stated confidence on the x-axis against your empirical accuracy on the y-axis. For a perfectly calibrated forecaster, this is the 45° diagonal — at every confidence level, accuracy matches confidence. Overconfidence shows up as a curve **below** the diagonal: you claim 80% confidence but hit at 50%. Underconfidence shows up as a curve **above** the diagonal: you claim 50% confidence but hit at 80%.

Most people's calibration curves look like a shallow S-shape: at very low confidence, they are slightly underconfident (things they claim are "improbable" turn out to never happen); at moderate confidence, they are overconfident; at very high confidence, they are again slightly better calibrated (things they call "almost certain" often are).

**Brier score decomposition ([Day 60](./day-60-decision-hygiene.md) revisited).** The Brier score breaks into three parts: how well-calibrated you are (Reliability — lower is better), how well you distinguish outcomes from non-outcomes (Resolution — higher is better), and the base-rate variance of what you're predicting (Uncertainty — a constant you don't control). A forecaster who always predicts the base rate has perfect reliability but zero resolution — they're never miscalibrated because they never stick their neck out, but they're also never useful. A forecaster who perfectly predicts every outcome has both perfect reliability and perfect resolution. In practice, making more extreme predictions improves resolution but tends to hurt reliability unless your information is genuinely strong. Your goal is to push resolution as high as you can while keeping reliability low — which means making specific, confident predictions only when your information genuinely warrants it.

**Bayesian update in full.** Bayes' rule in everyday language: your new belief in a hypothesis equals your old belief multiplied by how much more likely you think the evidence would be if the hypothesis were true compared to if it were false. The convenient way to track this is in odds rather than probabilities:

> **Posterior odds = Prior odds × likelihood ratio**

where the likelihood ratio is how much more probable the evidence is under your hypothesis than under the alternative. Worked example: you believe there's a 20% chance a project will be delivered on time (prior odds = 1:4). You learn that a key dependency was resolved two weeks ahead of schedule. You estimate this signal is 5× more likely if the project will finish on time than if it will be late (likelihood ratio = 5). Posterior odds = 5:4, which means P(on time | signal) = 5/9 ≈ 56%. A 20% prior becomes a 56% posterior on a single informative signal.

This is why strong evidence should produce large updates — and why resisting the update (staying near 20% because that "feels more prudent") is miscalibration, not humility.

## Where It Breaks

**Calibration requires feedback, and feedback requires resolution.**

You can only calibrate on predictions that resolve — events that have clear, observable outcomes within a reasonable timeframe. Short-horizon predictions (1–4 weeks) resolve quickly and build calibration fast. Long-horizon predictions (10-year career outcomes, 20-year geopolitical trends) are hard to calibrate directly because by the time you get feedback, your prediction-making habits may have changed substantially. The practical implication: train calibration on short-horizon predictions, then deliberately transfer the skill to longer horizons by decomposing them into shorter-horizon sub-predictions.

**Domain specificity.**

A superforecaster in geopolitics is not automatically calibrated about medical research or personal finance. Calibration is domain-specific because base rates, evidence quality, and the structure of relevant information differ across domains. A well-calibrated weather forecaster has decades of feedback on a domain with regular, unambiguous resolution and known physical regularities. That calibration does not automatically transfer to economics, which has much noisier signals and longer resolution horizons. Train calibration in the specific domains that matter for your decisions, not just in whichever domain provides the most convenient feedback.

**The confidence-certainty social norm.**

Well-calibrated forecasters often say things like "I'm about 55% confident this will work" — which, in many professional contexts, is read as uncertainty and weakness. The cultural expectation is that confident professionals project certainty. This creates pressure to inflate stated confidence, which corrupts the calibration signal.

The antidote is gradual norm change through demonstrating that probabilistic reasoning is more useful than false certainty — "I said 60% and it happened, here's what I learned" is more compelling over time than "I was confident and it worked, trust my judgment." This is a long game, and it requires finding professional contexts that reward epistemic honesty rather than punishing it.

## Try It Yourself

**Exercise 1 — Calibration quiz.**

For each question below, give a 90% confidence interval. You should be 90% confident the true value falls within your stated range. Don't make the ranges trivially wide — the exercise only works if you try to be genuinely tight.

(a) Year in which Amazon was founded
(b) Number of bones in the adult human body
(c) Distance from Earth to Moon in kilometres (average)
(d) Year in which India gained independence from British rule
(e) Nominal GDP of Germany in USD (most recent available year)

After answering, check the true values. Score: how many of your five intervals contained the true answer? Target: 4 to 5 out of 5. If you scored 2 or fewer, you are significantly overconfident — this is the norm, not the exception, on a first attempt.

**Exercise 2 — Fermi estimation.**

Estimate the number of software engineers currently employed in your country. Show your full decomposition (do not just guess a single number — break it down into steps). State your 90% confidence interval. After completing the decomposition, check against the Bureau of Labor Statistics (for the US), ONS (for the UK), or the equivalent national statistics body for your country. Was your CI correct? If not, which step in your decomposition was most wrong?

**Exercise 3 — Prediction log, initialised.**

Make 5 predictions about events that will resolve within 30 days. Choose events that are specific and have clear, unambiguous resolution criteria (avoid vague predictions). For each:
1. Describe the event precisely (who, what, by when)
2. State your probability (0–100%)
3. Explain your reasoning in 2 sentences — including which reference class you consulted
4. Set a calendar reminder to record the outcome on the resolution date

After the 30 days, compute your Brier score: (1/5) × Σ(fᵢ − oᵢ)². A score below 0.20 is good for a first attempt. Use the result to identify your calibration direction (over- or underconfident) and adjust your stated confidence levels accordingly in the next 30-day batch.

## Connect It Back

Calibration is the skill that makes all other decision tools operational. Without calibration, your probability estimates are fiction — they have the form of precision without the substance. With calibration, they are a genuine representation of your epistemic state, and the full arsenal of decision theory, Bayesian reasoning, and expected value calculation becomes actionable.

Consider what has accumulated over this module: in Day 56 you mapped your career as a strategic game; Day 57 added the network structure governing information and opportunity; Day 58 gave you commitment devices for managing your future self; Day 59 applied bargaining theory to negotiation; Day 60 gave you the structural practices of decision hygiene; and today's calibration practice trains the probabilistic faculty that underlies all of it.

The connection to calibration is immediate: portfolio calculations are only as good as the probability estimates they use.
