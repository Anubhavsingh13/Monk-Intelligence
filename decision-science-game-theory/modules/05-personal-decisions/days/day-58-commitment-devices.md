# Day 58 — Commitment Devices & Self-Control

**Module 5: Personal Decision-Making**
**Reading time: ~35 minutes**

---

**Today's one idea:** Time-inconsistent preferences — wanting to eat healthily tomorrow but junk food today — create an internal Prisoner's Dilemma between your present self and your future self, and commitment devices solve this by making present-you bind future-you to the strategy that serves your long-run interests.

## The Hook

Odysseus asked his crew to tie him to the mast before sailing past the Sirens.

He had heard stories of the Sirens' song — that it was beautiful beyond resistance, that sailors who heard it steered toward the rocks before they knew what they were doing. Present-Odysseus, thinking clearly before the song, knew that future-Odysseus, hearing the song, would order the ship to turn. He couldn't trust future-Odysseus to make the right decision in the moment of temptation.

So he solved the problem in advance. He instructed his crew to lash him to the mast and plug their own ears with wax. He ordered them not to release him no matter what he said — and if he ordered them to turn the ship, to bind him tighter. Then he removed future-Odysseus's ability to choose.

This is the original commitment device. You make your future self's choice in advance, from a position of clear thinking, and then structure the situation so your future self cannot undo it when the temptation arrives.

The game theory of self-control is the game theory of an interaction between two players with different preferences: present-you and future-you. They share a body and a set of experiences, but they weight costs and benefits differently. Present-you discounts the future steeply. Future-you (when it becomes present-you) will also discount its future steeply. This asymmetry generates inconsistent preferences across time — and those inconsistent preferences create strategic interaction between your selves.

You already know how to think about strategic interaction. You've spent five modules on it. Now apply it inward.

## Building the Intuition

### 1. Time Inconsistency — The Internal Prisoner's Dilemma

Standard economic models assume exponential discounting: you value tomorrow's benefit at δ times today's, and next week's at δ^7 times today's. This creates consistent preferences: what you prefer today is what you will prefer tomorrow, adjusted for the passage of time.

Real human discounting is hyperbolic. People discount the near future steeply and the far future less steeply. This creates a specific distortion: you are highly impatient for things that are imminent and relatively patient about things that are distant.

The consequence: preferences reverse as the future approaches.

On Monday, you are asked: would you prefer a salad on Friday or cake on Friday? You choose the salad — it's in the future, health seems important from this distance. On Friday, you are asked: would you prefer a salad now or cake now? You choose the cake — it's in front of you, the immediate pleasure outweighs the distant health benefit.

Same choice. Same person. Different preferences. This is time inconsistency.

The internal game: present-you chooses an action that binds future-you. Future-you, given free choice, would make a different decision than present-you is making on its behalf. This is a genuine 2-player game between your selves across time, with conflicting interests.

**Backward induction (Day 21) and self-knowledge:** a sophisticated agent uses backward induction to correctly anticipate future-you's preferences. Knowing that future-you will want cake when it's in front of them, present-you designs a pre-commitment that prevents future-you from accessing the cake. This is sophisticated self-regulation: using strategic thinking about your own future behaviour to constrain it in advance.

A naïve agent believes future-you will have the same preferences as present-you. Present-you makes a plan — "I will eat the salad on Friday" — and genuinely believes future-you will follow it. Future-you doesn't. The naïve agent makes the same plans and fails to follow them repeatedly, without updating the model of future-you.

**Which are you?** Most people are somewhere between fully sophisticated and fully naïve. Research suggests people know they're time-inconsistent but systematically underestimate how much. They are, in David Laibson's term, "partially naïve." The key diagnostic: if you have failed to follow the same plan before (exercise routine, diet, work habits, savings), future-you will likely fail again. Design for the actual future-you, not the idealised version.

The internal Prisoner's Dilemma framing: if present-you and future-you were separate agents, they would both prefer the outcome where both selves cooperate (follow the plan). But present-you defects by choosing the option that feels better now, and future-you defects by abandoning the plan when the moment arrives. Both selves end up in a worse equilibrium than the cooperative outcome would have produced. Commitment devices are the mechanism that enforces cooperation between your selves.

### 2. The Three Types of Commitment Devices

Not all commitment devices work the same way. They vary in how binding they are, how reversible, and what costs they impose on defection.

**Hard constraints: remove the option entirely.**

The most effective commitment removes the tempting choice from the choice set. Locking money in a pension with early-withdrawal penalties means the cake isn't on the table. Deleting social media apps from your phone means the distraction isn't one tap away. Giving your exam proctor your phone for the duration of the exam means you can't check it.

Hard constraints are powerful because they don't require willpower at the moment of temptation — the temptation is not present. Willpower is a depleting resource (the evidence on ego depletion is mixed, but the intuition is directionally correct — resisting temptation is effortful). A hard constraint removes the need for willpower entirely.

The limitation: hard constraints are often irreversible or require significant friction to undo. This is a feature if you're committing to the right thing and a bug if you're committing to the wrong thing.

**Costly actions: make defection expensive.**

If you can't remove the option, make it costly to choose. Telling a group of friends you will donate $100 to a cause you find objectionable if you fail to complete your weekly run makes not running expensive — not physically impossible, but costly enough to deter casual defection.

Beeminder is the formalised version: you set a goal and a monetary penalty for missing it, paid to Beeminder. The social contract and financial cost combine to raise the defection threshold. Websites like stickK let you designate the anti-charity penalty and assign a referee to verify your compliance.

The strength of this mechanism depends on calibrating the penalty correctly. Too small: doesn't deter casual defection. Too large: creates anxiety and may trigger the cancellation of the commitment itself. And critically: you must set the penalty when you're thinking clearly (present-you), not at the moment of temptation (future-you).

**Environmental design: change the effort asymmetry.**

The third type doesn't impose costs or remove options — it tilts the playing field. By changing your environment, you make the desired action easier and the undesired action harder.

Keeping unhealthy food out of the house doesn't make it impossible to eat junk food (you could order it or go out). It raises the effort required. Installing website blockers on your computer doesn't make distraction impossible. It adds friction. Putting your gym bag by the door the night before doesn't make you go — but it removes one small obstacle in a chain of small obstacles.

Environmental design works because the effortfulness of an action affects whether people take it. Even small friction reduces uptake substantially (this is the nudge architecture from Day 52). Present-you, acting at night when thinking is clear, can redesign the environment to make it easier for future-you to make good choices. This is architecturing your own choice environment — applying the same logic that nudge designers use for populations to your individual situation.

### 3. "Save More Tomorrow" (Thaler & Benartzi 2004)

The most elegant demonstration of commitment design in the wild.

**The problem:** most people report wanting to save more for retirement. Most people don't actually save more when asked. Standard approaches — education campaigns, financial advice, automatic enrolment — have modest effects. Present bias means that saving feels painful today (you reduce current consumption) even though the present-you genuinely wants the future-you to have more money.

**The SMarT (Save More Tomorrow) solution:** instead of asking people to save more now, ask them to commit to saving a fraction of future pay raises. When your salary goes up next year, a preset fraction goes to savings automatically.

Why does this work? Three interlocking mechanisms.

First, present bias makes it easy to commit future income. Future raises are temporally distant and feel costless. Committing to save a fraction of a raise you haven't received yet costs present-you almost nothing — there is no immediate consumption sacrifice. The commitment is made from a position of clear thinking, before temptation arrives.

Second, loss aversion is avoided. Because savings are taken from raises rather than from current pay, participants never see the money in their paycheck. They can't lose what they never had. From Day 8 (prospect theory): loss aversion is triggered when a reference point is established and then taken away. SMarT avoids establishing the reference point in the first place.

Third, automatic default means inertia works for saving instead of against it. Once enrolled, participants stay enrolled — the same status quo bias (Day 52) that prevents people from increasing their savings rate in conventional programmes now keeps them in the SMarT programme.

**The outcome:** participants increased their savings rate from 3.5% to 13.6% over 28 months without any subjective sense of deprivation. The device worked not by changing preferences but by aligning the timing of commitment (present, clear-headed) with the timing of sacrifice (future, when it no longer hurts).

This is commitment device design at its most sophisticated: it targets the specific psychological failure mode (present bias), avoids the specific psychological barrier (loss aversion), and uses the existing failure mode (inertia/defaults) to enforce the commitment.

### 4. Precommitment in Professional Life

The SMarT logic applies far beyond retirement savings.

**Deadlines as commitment devices (Ariely & Wertenbroch 2002):** Ariely and Wertenbroch gave students in a class three options for when to set assignment deadlines: all at the end of the semester, evenly spaced through the semester, or freely chosen. Students who chose evenly-spaced deadlines (with penalties for missing them) performed significantly better than those with a single end-of-semester deadline. Present-you can bargain with future-you through deadline structure — the intermediate deadline commits future-you to work before the final crunch.

The result is particularly striking because the students chose their own intermediate deadlines — they weren't imposed. Sophisticated students, anticipating their own procrastination, voluntarily constrained themselves. This is backward induction applied to your own procrastination habits.

**Public commitments:** from Day 24 (Schelling), making a commitment public makes it more costly to abandon. The loss of face from publicly failing a goal is a real social cost. Telling your team you will have the analysis done by Thursday is a stronger commitment than privately intending to have it done by Thursday. Announcing a project, career pivot, or goal to a professional audience creates accountability that is absent from private goals.

This is also a costly signal (Day 28): publicly committing to a difficult goal is a signal of genuine intent — because it would be costly for someone who doesn't intend to follow through to make the public commitment (the social cost of public failure).

**Accountability partners:** an external monitor who checks whether you've met your commitment adds a social cost to defection. Failing your commitment in front of an accountability partner converts the internal self-control problem into a reputation problem (Day 56). You know how to solve reputation problems: the Folk Theorem tells us that when δ is high enough and defection has consequences, cooperation is sustainable. Accountability partnerships succeed partly because they invoke this repeated-game mechanism.

**"If-then" planning (implementation intentions):** pre-specifying your response to anticipated temptation: "If I am at a restaurant and feel like ordering dessert, then I will drink water instead." Research on implementation intentions consistently shows they improve follow-through relative to goal intentions alone ("I want to eat more healthily"). The mechanism: pre-specification reduces the cognitive load at the moment of choice. Future-you, in the moment of temptation, does not need to deliberate. The decision has already been made.

Implementation intentions are low-cost hard constraints: they don't remove the option, but they pre-commit to a specific response that makes the tempting option less salient.

### 5. The Game Theory of Self-Commitment

The formal structure is a game between present-you (player 1) and future-you (player 2), played sequentially.

Present-you moves first: choose to commit or not commit. If present-you commits, future-you's choice set is constrained. If present-you doesn't commit, future-you has full choice set.

Future-you moves second: given the choice set available, maximise utility from future-you's perspective (which includes the present bias that discounts future-future consequences).

**Backward induction (Day 21):** a sophisticated present-you uses backward induction to anticipate future-you's choices. In each scenario (commit / don't commit), what will future-you do? Present-you chooses the action that produces the best outcome according to present-you's preferences, knowing what future-you will do in each case.

If present-you can correctly model future-you's preferences — specifically, can correctly estimate the magnitude of present bias β — then the commitment device can be calibrated precisely. Strong enough to deter casual defection; flexible enough for genuine emergencies.

**The calibration problem:** the hardest part of commitment device design is not the mechanics — it's correctly estimating future-you's temptation. If you underestimate your procrastination, you set deadlines too late and they don't bind. If you overestimate your temptation, you set penalties too high and the commitment becomes stressful or gets cancelled. Correct self-knowledge is a prerequisite for effective self-commitment.

The evidence suggests most people slightly underestimate their future temptations — they are partially naïve. The practical correction: if you've tried a similar commitment before and failed, calibrate your estimate of future-you's weakness upward. Design for the future-you who has failed this specific commitment before, not the future-you who never has.

### 6. When Commitment Devices Backfire

**Overcorrection removes useful flexibility.** A commitment rigid enough that you cannot adapt to genuinely new information trades time-inconsistency for excessive rigidity. If you commit to "never accept a meeting that isn't 48 hours notice" and a genuinely important opportunity arises with short notice, your hard commitment is now a cost. The goal is to bind yourself against temptation, not against all judgment. Well-designed commitment devices have escape hatches — but make them expensive enough that casual defection is deterred.

**Committing to the wrong metric.** From Day 46 (Goodhart's Law): when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. If your real goal is "write a high-quality paper" and your commitment device is "write 1000 words per day," you may produce 1000 words of low-quality filler to satisfy the commitment while missing the real goal. Commitment devices inherit Goodhart's Law problems whenever the commitment metric diverges from the true goal. Design commitments around output quality, not just quantity.

**Identity vs. behaviour.** The most durable commitments change your identity, not just your behaviour. "I am a person who exercises" is more robust against future-self defection than "I have committed to exercise three times per week." The identity-based commitment is not mediated by the same present-bias mechanism: it's not "do I want to exercise today?" but "what would a person like me do?" James Clear's *Atomic Habits* framework makes this point directly: the most powerful habit formation occurs when the habit is connected to a self-concept.

Identity-based commitment is a form of commitment device that operates through self-concept rather than through constraints or costs. It's slower to build but more intrinsically maintained — and not susceptible to the same calibration problems as external commitment mechanisms.

**Autonomy concerns.** There is a meaningful distinction between commitment devices you design for yourself and nudges designed by platforms or employers for you. Self-designed commitments preserve your autonomy — you choose the constraint because you predict you will thank yourself later. Externally imposed commitment-like structures (default enrolment in programmes, choice architectures that steer you toward preferred options) raise questions about whose preferences are being served (Day 52). This doesn't mean external nudges are wrong — the SMarT programme produces outcomes that participants themselves endorse. But the ethical line matters: commitment devices are most defensible when they are self-designed and self-chosen.

## The Formal Picture

**Hyperbolic discounting.** Standard economic models value future payoffs by multiplying today's value by a fixed discount factor for each period that passes — so next year is worth δ times this year, the year after is worth δ² times this year, and so on. This generates consistent preferences: whatever you prefer today, you'll still prefer it tomorrow (all else equal), because the future just shifts one period closer. Real human discounting doesn't work this way. People apply a steep penalty to anything that's in the near future and a smaller, more patient discount to anything far away. This is hyperbolic discounting — and it generates preference reversals. What feels like a reasonable sacrifice when it's a month away feels unbearable when it's today.

**Quasi-hyperbolic (β-δ) discounting (Laibson 1997).** The model economists use to capture this has two parameters. δ is your long-run patience — how much you discount each additional year into the future, which stays roughly consistent over time (about 0.95 per year for most people). β is your present bias — an extra penalty applied to everything that isn't right now. Multiply β times what you'd get from exponential discounting and you get your actual preferences.

Empirical estimates: β ≈ 0.7 fits most data on retirement savings and consumer behaviour. If β = 1, there is no present bias — you have perfectly exponential preferences and no commitment problems. The further β falls below 1, the stronger your present bias and the more valuable a well-designed commitment device is. Exercise 3 uses β = 0.6 and β = 0.4 to walk through concrete calculations.

**Commitment value.** A commitment device that successfully binds future-you to the strategy your clear-headed self prefers is worth:

> **value of commitment = β × (what future-you achieves with commitment − what future-you achieves without it)**

The value scales with your present bias (β). High present bias means a large gap between what future-you would do freely (give in to temptation) and what future-you does when bound by the commitment (follow the plan). Low present bias means future-you rarely gives in anyway, so the commitment adds little. This is why the same commitment device helps some people enormously and barely moves the needle for others — it depends entirely on how strong their present bias is for that specific temptation.

## Where It Breaks

**Commitment requires correct self-knowledge.** You must correctly anticipate which future temptations you will face and how strong they will be. If you misspecify the temptation — either under- or over-estimating it — the commitment device is either too weak (doesn't bind when it should) or too rigid (binds when you have genuinely good reasons to change course). Accurate self-knowledge is a prerequisite for accurate commitment design, and systematic underestimation of future temptation is common.

**External commitment is fragile to your own renegotiation.** You can install website blockers and then uninstall them. You can tell your accountability partner you're abandoning the goal. You can close the high-fee savings account. The most effective commitments are either irreversible (pension lock-in, the tattoo, the burned boat) or social (removing them requires public embarrassment). Pure self-commitments — where only you monitor compliance — are the weakest. The more observable and social the commitment, the harder it is to quietly undo.

**Commitment devices solve one failure mode and can create others.** A financial commitment device (pay penalty if you miss a goal) solves present bias. It can create anxiety, perfectionism, or counter-productive stress if calibrated too aggressively. Always ask: does this device address the actual failure mode, and does it create new ones?

**The β-δ model assumes preference stability.** Your $\beta$ and $\delta$ parameters are assumed stable over time. But in reality, your time preference varies — with stress levels, physical health, financial pressure, and life stage. A commitment designed for your normal-life $\beta$ may be wrong for a high-stress period. Building in review points (reassess the commitment structure every quarter) helps maintain calibration.

## Try It Yourself

**Exercise 1: Self-diagnosis**

Identify one area of your life where you consistently fail to follow through on plans you make in advance — exercise, diet, work habits, financial savings, creative projects, professional development.

First: is the failure due to time inconsistency (you change your mind as the moment approaches) or a genuine capability gap (you don't know how to do the thing)? Commitment devices solve time inconsistency, not capability gaps.

If it's time inconsistency: which of the three device types fits best?
- Hard constraint (remove the option)
- Costly action (make defection expensive)
- Environmental design (change the effort asymmetry)

What specifically would you design? And how would you verify compliance?

**Exercise 2: Design a professional commitment device**

Design a commitment device for a specific professional goal — for example: "submit a conference paper by the deadline" or "complete the project scope document by end of month" or "have a difficult conversation with a colleague this week."

Specify:
1. The binding mechanism (what does the commitment look like in practice?)
2. What happens if you defect (what is the cost, and who observes it?)
3. Whether it is public (and if so, to whom)
4. How you would verify compliance (who checks? when?)
5. Whether there is an escape hatch for genuine emergencies (and what qualifies)

Evaluate: is the cost of defection high enough to deter casual delay, but low enough that you won't cancel the whole commitment when life gets hard?

**Exercise 3: β-δ calculation**

You have a task worth $V = 10$ to your long-run self when completed. It requires effort $c = 3$ today to begin.

With $\beta = 0.6$, $\delta = 0.95$ (quasi-hyperbolic discounting):

**Utility of doing it today:** you incur cost −3 now and get payoff +10 now.
U(today) = −3 + 10 = **7**

**Utility of doing it tomorrow (discounted back to today's perspective):**
Tomorrow's net payoff is 10 − 3 = 7. From today's perspective, this is discounted by β × δ:
U(tomorrow, seen from today) = β × δ × 7 = 0.6 × 0.95 × 7 = **3.99**

Since 7 > 3.99, present-you prefers to do it today. Good.

But now ask: when you wake up tomorrow and recalculate, what does future-you prefer?
U(do it that same day) = −3 + 10 = **7**
U(wait one more day, seen from tomorrow) = β × δ × 7 = **3.99**

Tomorrow-you also prefers to do it today (i.e., that same tomorrow). Good — the task gets done.

Now change the parameters: β = 0.4, effort c = 5, payoff V = 10.

Redo the calculation. Does today-you prefer to do it today or tomorrow? Does tomorrow-you prefer to do it that day or the day after? At what value of $\beta$ does procrastination start? What would a commitment device with an immediate external penalty $p$ for not starting today do to the calculation — what minimum $p$ would be needed to make today-you start?

## Connect It Back

Commitment devices are the personal governance layer of everything you've learned.

The career reputation game (Day 56) requires consistent behaviour over time — commitment devices are what make consistent behaviour achievable when present bias pulls you toward shortcuts. The network strategy (Day 57) requires regular maintenance of weak ties — a commitment device (a quarterly calendar reminder, a small social obligation) converts this into reliable behaviour. The long games in career and life require patience — and patience is exactly what commitment devices manufacture for us by binding future-you before temptation arrives.

- Reputation (Day 56): how to be trusted across time
