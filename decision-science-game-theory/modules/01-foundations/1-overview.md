# Module 1 — The Science of Choosing
*Days 1–13 · Individual decisions under uncertainty and bias*

---

## What This Module Earns You

By the end of Day 13 you will have a complete toolkit for making and analysing *individual* decisions — decisions where you are the only strategic actor. You will be able to:

- Recognise which cognitive system is driving a decision and interrupt it when necessary.
- Compute expected value and expected utility, and understand when each applies.
- Update beliefs rationally using Bayes' theorem — in your head, on paper, and in code.
- Name and anticipate the 8 most consequential cognitive biases.
- Decide when gathering more information is worth the cost, and when to act now.
- Structure a multi-stage decision as a tree and solve it backward.
- Handle decisions where probabilities themselves are unknown.

This module is the foundation everything else stands on. Game theory (Module 2) is built on a model of rational players — but only this module tells you what "rational" actually means and where it breaks. Reinforcement learning (Module 4) is applied sequential decision-making — but only this module gives you the vocabulary to see it that way.

---

## The Story of This Module

Most people believe they are worse at making decisions than experts — and they're right, but not for the reason they think. The problem is not lack of information or intelligence. It is that the cognitive machinery we use to decide was built for a world of immediate, concrete, social threats — not for evaluating probabilities, discounting future payoffs, or reasoning about counterfactuals.

This module does two things simultaneously: it builds the normative model (what a rational agent *should* do) and the descriptive model (what humans *actually* do). These two models are in constant dialogue. Every bias in Days 6–8 is a departure from the normative model built in Days 2–5. Prospect Theory (Day 8) is not a failure story — it is a richer, more accurate model that replaces expected utility as the *descriptive* account of choice.

By Day 13, you will be fluent in both models — and know which to use when.
