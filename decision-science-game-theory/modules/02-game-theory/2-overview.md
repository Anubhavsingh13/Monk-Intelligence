# Module 2 — Game Theory Fundamentals
*Days 14–27 · Strategic interaction between rational agents*

---

## What This Module Earns You

By the end of Day 27 you will have moved from thinking about decisions in isolation to reasoning about decisions in the presence of other strategic actors. You will be able to:

- Represent any strategic situation as a formal game and identify its solution concept.
- Find Nash equilibria in pure and mixed strategies.
- Predict outcomes in sequential games using backward induction.
- Explain why individually rational behaviour can be collectively catastrophic.
- Analyse why cooperation emerges in repeated interactions and when it fails.
- Apply bargaining theory to salary negotiations, partnerships, and product decisions.
- Understand auction formats, the Revenue Equivalence Theorem, and bidding strategy.
- Reason about markets where one party knows more than another.

---

## The Story of This Module

Module 1 treated you as the only decision-maker in the world. That was a useful simplification — but almost no important real-world decision has just one player. The moment there are two or more people with conflicting or overlapping interests, the game changes fundamentally: the best action now depends on what others do, and they are reasoning about you reasoning about them.

The central tool is the Nash equilibrium — a prediction of what rational players will do in any strategic situation. It is not always the *best* outcome (the Prisoner's Dilemma proves that), but it is the *stable* one — no one has a reason to deviate unilaterally. Understanding when Nash equilibria are efficient and when they are not is the key to mechanism design (Module 3) and AI product incentive architecture (Module 4).

The module ends at information asymmetry (Day 27) — the gateway into the mechanism design arc. This is the moment the question changes from "given the rules, how do I play?" to "given that people will play strategically, what rules should I design?"
