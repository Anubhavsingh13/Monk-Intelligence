## Module 1 — The Science of Choosing (Days 1–13)

| Day | Page Title | Load-Bearing Idea | Why It Comes Now | Prereqs |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 1 | [How Decisions Actually Happen](modules/01-foundations/days/day-01-how-decisions-actually-happen.md) | Two parallel systems drive every choice — fast pattern-matching and slow deliberation — and they routinely conflict. | Sets the mental model for the entire course: we are not rational calculators. | None |
| 2 | [Expected Value — The Rational Baseline](modules/01-foundations/days/day-02-expected-value.md) | The "correct" choice under uncertainty is the one that maximises the probability-weighted average outcome. | Before studying how we deviate from rationality, you need to internalise the rational benchmark. | Day 1 |
| 3 | [Expected Utility — Why $2M Isn't Twice $1M](modules/01-foundations/days/day-03-expected-utility.md) | Rational agents maximise expected *utility*, not expected *value* — because satisfaction is a curved function of outcomes. | Bridges EV to real behaviour; explains why risk aversion is coherent, not irrational. | Day 2 |
| 4 | [Probability & Calibration](modules/01-foundations/days/day-04-probability-calibration.md) | A well-calibrated forecaster assigns probabilities that match actual frequencies — most people are systematically overconfident. | Probability is the language of uncertainty; you need fluency before Bayesian reasoning. | Day 1 |
| 5 | [Bayesian Updating — Beliefs as Living Things](modules/01-foundations/days/day-05-bayesian-updating.md) | New evidence should shift your prior belief in exact proportion to how much more likely it makes the evidence. | The single most important quantitative tool in the course; anchors all later AI content. | Days 2, 4 |
| 6 | [Cognitive Biases I — Perception Traps](modules/01-foundations/days/day-06-cognitive-biases-i.md) | Anchoring, availability, and framing systematically distort how we perceive options before we even begin reasoning. | Names the failure modes of System 1 so we can recognise and interrupt them. | Day 1 |
| 7 | [Cognitive Biases II — Motivation Traps](modules/01-foundations/days/day-07-cognitive-biases-ii.md) | Loss aversion, sunk-cost fallacy, and status-quo bias warp decisions *after* perception — through the lens of what we stand to lose. | Completes the bias taxonomy; loss aversion directly connects to Prospect Theory tomorrow. | Day 6 |
| 8 | [Prospect Theory — The Behavioural Map of Choice](modules/01-foundations/days/day-08-prospect-theory.md) | People evaluate outcomes as gains/losses from a reference point, weighting losses roughly twice as heavily as equivalent gains. | The field's landmark empirical finding; replaces Expected Utility as a *descriptive* model. | Days 3, 7 |
| 9 | [The Value of Information](modules/01-foundations/days/day-09-value-of-information.md) | The value of learning something new equals the improvement it creates in your best decision, discounted by its cost. | Teaches when to gather more data vs. act now — critical for both AI and life decisions. | Days 2, 5 |
| 10 | [Decision Trees & Influence Diagrams](modules/01-foundations/days/day-10-decision-trees.md) | A decision tree converts a messy sequential choice into a structured diagram you can solve backward. | The primary practical tool for structuring multi-stage decisions with uncertainty. | Days 2, 5, 9 |
| 11 | **[R&S — Module 1 Consolidation](modules/01-foundations/days/day-11-rest-synthesise.md)** | Re-derive EV, EU, and Bayesian updating from scratch; apply to one real past decision. | Consolidation before the course pivots to strategic (multi-player) thinking. | Days 1–10 |
| 12 | [Multi-Criteria Decisions](modules/01-foundations/days/day-12-multi-criteria-decisions.md) | When outcomes have multiple attributes, structured weighting prevents arbitrary tradeoffs. | Real decisions have multiple dimensions; this day gives a principled tool for that. | Day 10 |
| 13 | [Decisions Under Deep Uncertainty](modules/01-foundations/days/day-13-deep-uncertainty.md) | When you can't assign probabilities, maximin, minimax regret, and robustness replace expected value. | Completes the decision-science arc; introduces the mindset for ambiguous AI deployment. | Days 2, 10 |

## Module 2 — Game Theory Fundamentals (Days 14–27)

| Day | Page Title | Load-Bearing Idea | Why It Comes Now | Prereqs |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 14 | [What Is a Game?](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-14-what-is-a-game.md) | A game is any situation where your best choice depends on what others choose — and they know that you know that. | Opens the multi-agent world; everything in Modules 2–3 builds from this definition. | Day 1 |
| 15 | [Dominant Strategies](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-15-dominant-strategies.md) | A strategy is dominant if it's your best move *regardless* of what others do — play it without needing to predict anyone. | Simplest solution concept; creates the ladder from easy cases to hard ones. | Day 14 |
| 16 | [Nash Equilibrium](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-16-nash-equilibrium.md) | A Nash equilibrium is a set of strategies where no single player can improve their outcome by switching unilaterally. | The foundational solution concept of game theory; all later equilibria extend or refine it. | Day 15 |
| 17 | [The Prisoner's Dilemma & Social Dilemmas](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-17-prisoners-dilemma.md) | Individually rational choices can produce collectively terrible outcomes. | The most famous game in existence; directly explains platform design failures and team dynamics. | Day 16 |
| 18 | [Mixed Strategies — When Randomness Is Rational](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-18-mixed-strategies.md) | In games with no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium, the optimal strategy is a deliberate randomisation over your options. | Completes Nash equilibrium theory; explains why pricing wars and penalty kicks look as they do. | Day 16 |
| 19 | [Sequential Games & Backward Induction](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-19-sequential-games.md) | In sequential games, you solve from the last move backward — anticipating what a rational opponent will do at each branch. | Pivots from simultaneous to time-structured games; introduces trees as strategy maps. | Days 16, 10 |
| 20 | [Commitment & Credible Threats](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-20-commitment-credible-threats.md) | A threat is only credible if you'd carry it out when the time comes — smart players design situations that make commitment visible. | One of game theory's deepest insights; explains negotiation, product strategy, and escalation. | Day 19 |
| 21 | [Repeated Games & the Emergence of Cooperation](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-21-repeated-games.md) | When a game is played repeatedly with the same partners, cooperation can become individually rational without external enforcement. | Explains how trust, reputation, and norms arise — essential for multi-agent AI systems. | Day 17 |
| 22 | **[R&S — Module 2 Mid-Point](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-22-rest-synthesise.md)** | Reconstruct the Prisoner's Dilemma, find its Nash equilibrium, then derive the repeated-game cooperation condition. | Mid-module consolidation before evolutionary and information-based extensions. | Days 14–21 |
| 23 | [Evolutionary Game Theory](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-23-evolutionary-game-theory.md) | Strategies that survive in a population are those that do well *against the current distribution* of strategies, not a rational opponent. | Reframes equilibrium as dynamic stability — critical for understanding how AI agents co-evolve. | Days 16, 21 |
| 24 | [Coordination Games & Focal Points](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-24-coordination-focal-points.md) | When multiple equilibria exist, players coordinate on the one that "stands out" — without communication. | Explains standards wars, platform lock-in, and why some conventions are arbitrary but sticky. | Day 16 |
| 25 | [Bargaining Theory — How Deals Get Made](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-25-bargaining-theory.md) | The Nash bargaining solution predicts deal terms from each party's disagreement payoff and patience. | Applied to salary negotiation, partnerships, and vendor contracts — core life skill with formal backing. | Days 16, 20 |
| 26 | [Auctions — Selling to the Highest Bidder](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-26-auctions.md) | The Revenue Equivalence Theorem says four common auction formats yield the same expected revenue — but differ in who wins. | Auctions appear everywhere in AI: ad systems, API pricing, resource allocation. | Days 16, 18 |
| 27 | [Information Asymmetry — When One Player Knows More](modules/02-game-theory/days/day-27-information-asymmetry.md) | Hidden information changes the game: the informed party can exploit; the uninformed party must screen or signal. | Gateway to mechanism design; explains insurance markets, hiring, and recommendation systems. | Days 16, 26 |

## Module 3 — Mechanism Design & Advanced Structures (Days 28–40)

| Day | Page Title | Load-Bearing Idea | Why It Comes Now | Prereqs |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 28 | [Signalling — Credible Communication](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-28-signalling.md) | A signal is credible only if it's too costly for the wrong type to mimic — cheap talk without commitment is ignored. | Explains education as credential, pricing as quality signal, and why AI outputs need verifiability. | Day 27 |
| 29 | [Screening & Adverse Selection](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-29-screening-adverse-selection.md) | A designer who can't observe types offers a menu so each type self-selects into the right contract. | The flip side of signalling; foundational for subscription tiers, API pricing, and personalisation. | Day 27 |
| 30 | [Mechanism Design — Engineering the Rules](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-30-mechanism-design.md) | Mechanism design asks: given that players will act strategically, what rules produce the outcome we want? | The "inverse game theory" that underlies every AI platform's incentive architecture. | Days 26, 27, 28, 29 |
| 31 | [Voting Systems & Arrow's Impossibility](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-31-voting-arrows-impossibility.md) | Arrow's Impossibility Theorem proves no ranked-choice system simultaneously satisfies four basic fairness criteria. | Directly relevant to multi-objective AI alignment and feature prioritisation across user segments. | Day 30 |
| 32 | [Public Goods & the Free-Rider Problem](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-32-public-goods-free-rider.md) | Markets underprovide public goods unless the mechanism internalises externalities. | Explains content moderation, open-source dynamics, and why AI safety is a coordination problem. | Days 17, 30 |
| 33 | [Network Effects & Two-Sided Markets](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-33-network-effects-platforms.md) | In a network with positive externalities, value grows super-linearly with users — creating winner-take-most dynamics. | Explains why AI product strategy is obsessed with data flywheels and switching costs. | Days 24, 33 |
| 34 | [Matching Markets — Who Gets What](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-34-matching-markets.md) | Stable matchings pair every agent such that no two unmatched agents prefer each other over their current match. | Used in residency matching, school choice, and recommendation systems. | Day 30 |
| 35 | **[R&S — Module 3 Mid-Point](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-35-rest-synthesise.md)** | Reconstruct the Revelation Principle from scratch; apply mechanism design to one product you know well. | Major consolidation before cooperative and computational extensions. | Days 14–34 |
| 36 | [The Shapley Value — Fair Division of Credit](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-36-shapley-value.md) | The Shapley value assigns each player exactly their average marginal contribution across all possible coalition orderings. | Directly used in SHAP explainability — the principled answer to "how much did each feature contribute?" | Day 16 |
| 37 | [Correlated Equilibrium — Beyond Nash](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-37-correlated-equilibrium.md) | A correlated equilibrium uses a trusted mediator to recommend strategies — it always exists and can Pareto-dominate Nash. | More realistic than Nash in many real-world settings; foundational for multi-agent AI coordination. | Days 16, 18 |
| 38 | [Price of Anarchy — The Cost of Selfishness](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-38-price-of-anarchy.md) | The Price of Anarchy measures how much efficiency is lost when selfish agents replace a central planner. | Bridges classical game theory and AI systems; explains why naive agent deployment degrades system welfare. | Days 16, 30, 37 |
| 39 | [Information Design & Bayesian Persuasion](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-39-information-design.md) | A sender can influence a receiver's actions by strategically choosing what information to reveal, without lying. | Unifies signalling, mechanism design, and Bayesian reasoning; explains algorithmic content curation. | Days 5, 28, 30 |
| 40 | **[R&S — Module 3 Capstone](modules/03-mechanism-design/days/day-40-rest-synthesise.md)** | Map a real AI product to its game-theoretic structure — identify equilibria and incentive failures. | Caps the entire game theory arc before pivoting to AI implementation. | Days 14–39 |

## Module 4 — Decision Science in AI Products (Days 41–55)

| Day | Page Title | Load-Bearing Idea | Why It Comes Now | Prereqs |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 41 | [Reinforcement Learning: The Framework](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-41-reinforcement-learning.md) | An RL agent is simply a decision-maker in a multi-step uncertain world, choosing actions to maximise cumulative reward. | Reframes RL as applied decision theory — the entire module reads as extensions of Module 1. | Days 10, 19 |
| 42 | [The Exploration-Exploitation Tradeoff](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-42-exploration-exploitation.md) | Every learning agent faces a fundamental tension: exploit what it knows now, or explore to learn something better. | The most ubiquitous tradeoff in AI product design — A/B testing, recommendations, search, pricing. | Day 41 |
| 43 | [Markov Decision Processes](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-43-markov-decision-processes.md) | An MDP captures sequential decision-making via states, actions, transitions, and rewards — and has a well-defined optimal policy. | The formal language of all model-based RL; every RL paper from here on speaks MDP. | Days 41, 42 |
| 44 | [Q-Learning & Temporal Difference Methods](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-44-q-learning.md) | Q-learning learns the optimal action-value function directly from experience, without a model of the environment. | The algorithmic heart of model-free RL; connects directly to how LLMs are fine-tuned with RLHF. | Day 43 |
| 45 | [Policy Gradient Methods](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-45-policy-gradients.md) | Instead of learning values, policy gradient methods directly optimise the probability of taking good actions. | Necessary for continuous action spaces and probabilistic policies — the foundation of modern RLHF. | Day 44 |
| 46 | [Reward Design & Goodhart's Law](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-46-reward-design.md) | Misspecified rewards produce agents that optimise the letter but not the spirit of what you wanted. | Named the central AI alignment problem; every AI product builder who uses RL will confront this. | Days 44, 45 |
| 47 | **[R&S — RL Toolkit (Days 41–46)](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-47-rest-synthesise.md)** | Consolidate the core RL toolkit: Bellman equations, TD learning, policy gradients, and reward design. | Mid-module consolidation; tests whether RL concepts are internalised vs. memorised. | Days 41–46 |
| 48 | [Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-48-multi-agent-rl.md) | When multiple RL agents share an environment, their joint behaviour forms a game — and standard RL convergence guarantees break. | Explains why multi-model pipelines and competitive AI agents require game-theoretic analysis. | Days 46, 21, 23 |
| 49 | [Recommendation Systems as Sequential Decision-Making](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-49-recommendation-systems.md) | A recommender is not a prediction problem but a sequential decision-making problem — optimising long-term user value, not next click. | Unifies RL, mechanism design, and information design into the most common AI feature class. | Days 46, 44, 33 |
| 50 | [RLHF & Constitutional AI](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-50-rlhf-alignment.md) | RLHF converts the alignment problem into preference learning — but the reward model is itself a proxy subject to Goodhart's Law. | Deepens the alignment treatment from Day 46 with sycophancy, Constitutional AI, and scalable oversight. | Days 46, 45, 27 |
| 51 | [Platform Mechanism Design](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-51-platform-mechanism-design.md) | Every algorithmic choice a platform makes — ranking, pricing, matching — is a mechanism that shapes which equilibrium the market reaches. | Applies Module 3 machinery directly to real AI infrastructure and product design. | Days 33, 30, 38 |
| 52 | [Nudge Design & Behavioural Mechanism Design](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-52-nudge-design.md) | A nudge changes the choice architecture to steer behaviour without restricting options or changing incentives. | Translates behavioural economics into product design — defaults, friction, salience, commitment devices. | Days 6, 7, 8 |
| 53 | [A/B Testing as Sequential Decision-Making](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-53-ab-testing.md) | A/B testing is a bandit problem in disguise — every experiment is an exploration-exploitation decision, and stopping rules matter. | Reframes the most common data-driven workflow in product terms of online learning. | Days 42, 5 |
| 54 | [AI Safety as a Coordination Problem](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-54-ai-safety-coordination.md) | The most important AI safety challenges — avoiding race dynamics, maintaining oversight — are coordination games, not purely technical problems. | Connects game theory, mechanism design, and MARL to existential-scale decision problems. | Days 21, 23, 38, 46 |
| 55 | **[R&S — Module 4 Capstone](modules/04-ai-products/days/day-55-rest-synthesise.md)** | Design a full decision-science audit of one AI feature — biases, reward risks, mechanism incentives, nudge architecture. | Caps the AI module; integrates all four prior modules into a practitioner's checklist. | Days 41–54 |
